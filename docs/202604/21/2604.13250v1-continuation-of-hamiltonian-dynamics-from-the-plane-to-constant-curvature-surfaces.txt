# Continuation of Hamiltonian dynamics from the plane to constant-curvature surfaces 

## Cristina Stoica Wilfrid Laurier University, Waterloo, Canada 

Keywords : symmetry, Lie algebra In¨ on¨ u–Wigner contraction, Hamiltonian dynamics, curved n-body prob-lems, continuation of local dynamics 

Abstract 

We investigate the deformation of symmetry on cotangent bundles from the Euclidean plane to two-dimensional constant-curvature surfaces and the continuation of local dynamics aspects in Hamiltonian systems. For a fixed curvature sign σ ∈ { +1 , −1}, the curved problem is set up either on the sphere ( σ = +1) or on the hyperbolic plane ( σ = −1), both with radius R = 1 /ε ,recovering flat space in the limit ε → 0. The symmetry of these spaces is taken into account by using the In¨ on¨ u–Wigner contraction of Lie algebras from so (3) or so (2 , 1) to se (2). We use Riemannian exponential coordinates centred at the North pole together with the pull-back the associated momentum map and the symplectic form. Within this geometric setting we use a local slice construction and prove the persistence from flat to curved spaces of non-degenerate relative equilibria and relative periodic orbits of general cotangent bundle Hamiltonian systems. We apply the resulting framework to the Newtonian n-body problem.

# 1 Introduction 

A natural question in mathematical physics is whether known solutions of a dynamical system persist when the underlying geometry is deformed. In the case of the Newtonian n-body problem, such a deformation replaces the Euclidean plane R2 with a constant-curvature surface M σR of radius R = 1 /ε , where σ = +1 corresponds to the sphere and σ = −1 to the hyperbolic plane, recovering the flat space in the limit ε → 0. The curved Newtonian n-body problem has attracted sustained attention over the past two decades; see for instance [Chernoivan & al. 1999, Diacu & al. 2012a, Diacu & al. 2012b, Diacu 2012c, Mart´ ınez & al. 2017, Diacu et al. 2018, Tibboel 2018, P´ erez-Chavela & al. 2020, Hern´ andez-Gardu˜ no & al. 2022, Bengochea & al. 2022, Zhu 2024, Begonchea & al. 2025], and the survey [Diacu 2013] for background and motivation. The central results of this paper are local continuation theorems. We prove that relative equilibria (RE), periodic solutions and relative periodic solutions (RPOs) of Hamiltonian systems defined on the Euclidean plane persist to the corresponding curved problem on M σ 

> 1/ε

for ε > 0 sufficiently small provided standard nondegeneracy conditions hold. As a special case, we apply our results to the Newtonian n-body problem. Thus, the classical three-body Lagrange and Euler RE, the (non-degenerate) regular n-gon RE, as well as the figure-eight choreography [Chenciner and Montgomery 2000], all satisfy the required hypotheses and continue to the corresponding constant-curvature surface. Our approach rests on three ingredients that together reduce the problem to a perturbative argument on a fixed symplectic manifold. The first is the use of Riemannian exponential coordinates centred at the North pole of M σ

> 1/ε

. In these coordinates the curved surface is described by a single chart covering all but the antipodal point in the spherical case and globally in the hyperbolic case. After pull-back by the exponential chart the metric and momentum map admit explicit ε-expansions, whereas the canonical symplectic form is ε-independent. The n-body curved Hamiltonian expands as Hε,σ = H0 + σε 2H2 + O(ε4), where H0 is the standard planar Newtonian Hamiltonian and H2 is the explicit first curvature correction. The second ingredient is an Lie algebra In¨ on¨ u–Wigner contraction [In¨ on¨ u & al. 1953] to se (2), a proce-dure employed as well in [Montaldi & al. 2013] in the case of point vortices. By introducing an ε-dependent Lie bracket on a fixed three-dimensional vector space that interpolates between so (3) or so (2 , 1) at ε > 0and se (2) at ε = 0. Note that for σ = +1 the symmetry group of the curved problem is SO (3), while for 

σ = −1 it is SO +(2 , 1). Thus, by employing the deformations of Lie algebra, the symmetry and associated momentum map on the curved surfaces vary smoothly with ε. Furthermore, rotations or boosts about hori-zontal axes converge to planar translations as the curvature vanishes. and the momentum map converges to the standard SE (2) momentum map. The third ingredient is a local slice construction that, given any compact collision-free set in the chart phase space, produces smoothly varying slices representing locally the symplectic reduced spaces. On these slices, the reduced symplectic forms, reduced Hamiltonians, reduced Hamiltonian vector fields, and reduced Poincar´ e maps all depend smoothly on ε. Consequently, the continuation results follow from standard implicit function theorem arguments applied to a Hamiltonian context; we refer to [Marsden 1992], [Holm & al. 2009] and [Meyer & al. 2009] for more background on Hamiltonian systems’ symmetry, reduction, and continuation methods. The technique of Lie algebra deformations was also employed by [Montaldi & al. 2013] in the case of point vortex dynamics on hyperbolic, Euclidean and spherical surfaces. The authors provide a unified picture of the symmetry via smooth deformations sl (2) → se (2) → so (3). They use stereographic projection to provide a uniform coordinate system for constant-curvature surfaces, with the symplectic form encoded via the conformal factor. Note that the phase space of n vortices on a surface M is the product M n, while in our case we consider cotangent bundle phase spaces T ∗(M n), as is appropriate for n-body problems. The present paper carries this constant-curvature viewpoint over to cotangent-bundle Hamiltonian systems and, in particular, to the Newtonian n-body problem. Finally, note that when [Montaldi & al. 2013] consider the continuation arguments for RE, they restrict to the rotational symmetry SO (2), while we study the general context of the full symmetry groups. Our paper generalises the work of [Bengochea & al. 2022]. There the authors prove the continuation of the Newtonian n-body problem considering the rotational SO (2) symmetry only. They use stereographic 

2projection to provide a uniform coordinate system and, by employing a slice theorem, they prove the con-tinuation of central configurations (as base point configurations of RE). In their setting, the momentum map and the local slice model are independent of curvature and the persistence argument is applied to the RE condition only. In contrast, we consider the full symmetry groups, i.e. SE (2) on the plane and either SO (3) or SO +(2 , 1) on the curved surface, in the framework of Lie algebra deformations. After in-troducing the exponential (Riemannian normal) coordinates, we pull back the geometry and thereby deal with the ε-dependence of the symplectic form, momentum maps and the local slice model. We provide a general theorem applicable to any smooth family of Gε-invariant cotangent-bundle Hamiltonians of the form 

Hε = H0 + O(ε2), with the Newtonian n-body problem being treated as a case study. While developing the applications we noticed a less known fact: in the planar n-body problem, if the full symmetry group SE (2) is considered then an RE with non-zero rotational angular velocity must necessarily have zero translational momentum. So, for example, in the SE (2)-symmetric context, the Lagrangian triangular RE is not a RE unless its centre of mass is at rest in the origin of the chosen system of coordinates; otherwise, this “RE” is in fact an RPO. The paper is organised as follows. In Section 2 we develop the geometric framework: exponential coor-dinates, the Lie algebra In¨ on¨ u–Wigner contractions, group actions, the momentum map, and the symplectic form. Section 3 contains the main results: the local reduction theory and the continuation theorems. In Section 4 we present the explicit Hamiltonian expansion for the n-body problem and apply our results to specific configurations.

## 4.3 Application to the n-body problem RE and RPOs 

Recall that in the classical (Newtonian) planar n-body problem no equilibria are possible, due to the attrac-tive binary interaction. By Proposition 10, any planar RE with nonzero angular velocity ω̸ = 0 has zero total linear momentum, so its momentum value takes the form μ0 = (0 , 0, L ) ∈ se (2) ∗. For such momentum values, the isotropy subgroup is Gμ0 = SO (2) . Thus nondegeneracy on a Gμ0 -slice (i.e. Definition 2) means nondegeneracy of the Hessian of the reduced Hamiltonian at the projected equilibrium on the SO (2)-reduced space. Theorem 7 applies to any planar Newtonian RE that is nondegenerate on its SO(2)-slice: such an RE continues smoothly to a family of RE of the curved problem on M σ 

> 1/ε

for ε > 0 sufficiently small. Theorem 9 covers drifting configurations. As explained in Remark 10, a planar RE in the translation-reduced problem with non-zero total linear momentum ( μ1, μ 2)̸ = (0 , 0) lifts to an RPO of the SE (2) action: the configuration rotates about its centre of mass while the centre of mass drifts uniformly, returning to its original shape and orientation after one period T = 2 π/ω but displaced by ( μ1, μ 2)T /M . Provided the corresponding reduced periodic orbit is nondegenerate in the sense of Definition 5, Theorem 9 yields a smooth continuation of this RPO to ε > 0. Moreover, on M σ

> 1/ε

, the translational drift is replaced by a small curved-space isometric drift, as described in Remark 11. This applies to any nondegenerate planar RE: 

• Non-drifting configurations (μ1, μ 2) = (0 , 0) . Any nondegenerate RE with zero total linear momentum — including the Lagrange, Euler, and regular n-gon configurations RE nondegenerate modulo SO (2) — continues to a genuine RE on the curved surface via Theorem 7. 

• Drifting Lagrange configurations. Any nondegenerate RE with nonzero total linear momentum ( μ1, μ 2)̸ =(0 , 0) lifts to a drifting RPO of the full SE (2) action. Provided the reduced periodic orbit is nondegen-erate, Theorem 9 yields a smooth continuation to ε > 0: on M σ

> 1/ε

, the rigid configuration executes a near-periodic motion with a small rotational drift replacing the planar translational drift. This conclu-sion holds for any other nondegenerate drifting planar RE configuration, including drifting Lagrange or Euler RE and the regular n-gon configurations RE nondegenerate modulo SO (2). 

Application to the figure-eight choreography. In the planar Newtonian three-body problem with equal masses and centre of mass fixed, a special periodic solution (zero total angular momentum) is the figure-eight choreography [Chenciner and Montgomery 2000]. Roberts [Roberts 2007] proved that the orbit is linearly stable and non-resonant after removing the trivial neutral directions coming from the integrals and Euclidean symmetries; see also the numerical study of Floquet multipliers in [Berti & al. 2026] for further confirmation. Non-resonance means that no non-trivial Floquet multiplier of the reduced return map is a root of unity; in particular, no such multiplier equals 1, which is the nondegeneracy condition (69) on the reduced Poincar´ esection. Therefore Theorem 9 yields a local continuation of the figure-eight to ε > 0 sufficiently small as a nearby RPO, and Proposition 8 yields a corresponding continuation as a periodic orbit (identity drift), provided the orbit remains in the chart domain. 

# 5 Concluding remarks 

The continuation results established in this paper rest on three geometric ingredients: the exponential chart, the In¨ on¨ u–Wigner scaling of the symmetry, and the local slice construction of Lemma 6 . Together, these reduce all continuation questions to a fixed symplectic manifold on which standard implicit function theorem arguments apply. Our continuation theorems apply to any smooth family of Gε-invariant Hamiltonians of the form (59), and the Newtonian n-body problem is treated as the primary example. All results are 

18 local in nature, and are applicable to compact collision-free subsets of the exponential-chart phase space corresponding to configurations remaining in a bounded planar region. A natural further research direction concerns the behaviour near collisions. Since the metric on M σ 

> 1/ε

is locally Euclidean at small scales and curvature effects enter only at higher order, the regularised vector field near a collision at the North pole should be a C1-small perturbation of the corresponding planar regularised field. One therefore expects that the invariant sets arising in the planar three-body problem via blow-up techniques, as in the work of [McGehee 1974] and [Devaney 1980] to persist for ε sufficiently small. Making this precise requires verifying that the regularisation procedure commutes appropriately with the exponential chart and the curvature expansion. Finally, one may consider the numerical continuation of specific REs and RPOs and choreographies. [Bengochea & al. 2022] accomplished this for the Lagrangian RE in the SO (2) symmetric case. In our full symmetry context, the work of [C. Wulff 2008] may be relevant although the non-compactness of SE (2) may lead to additional challenges. These topics are left for future work.
