# Abstract 

Reliable celestial attitude determination is a critical re-quirement for autonomous spacecraft navigation, yet tra-ditional ”Lost-in-Space” (LIS) algorithms often suffer from high computational overhead and sensitivity to sensor-induced noise. While deep learning has emerged as a promising alternative, standard regression models are often confounded by the non-Euclidean topology of the celestial sphere and by the periodic boundary conditions of Right Ascension (RA) and Declination (Dec). In this paper, we present Star-Fusion , a multi-modal architec-ture that reformulates orientation estimation as a discrete topological classification task. Our approach leverages spherical K-Means cluster-ing to partition the celestial sphere into K topologi-cally consistent regions, effectively mitigating coordi-nate wrapping artifacts. The proposed architecture em-ploys a tripartite fusion strategy: a SwinV2-Tiny trans-former backbone for photometric feature extraction, a convolutional heatmap branch for spatial grounding, and a coordinate-based MLP for geometric anchoring. Ex-perimental evaluations on a synthetic Hipparcos -derived dataset demonstrate that Star-Fusion achieves a Top-1 ac-curacy of 93.4% and a Top-3 accuracy of 97.8%. Further-more, the model exhibits high computational efficiency, maintaining an inference latency of 18.4 ms on resource-constrained COTS hardware, making it a viable candi-date for real-time onboard deployment in next-generation satellite constellations. 

# 1 Introduction 

Spacecraft attitude determination, the process of calculat-ing a vehicle’s orientation relative to an inertial reference frame, is fundamental to mission success. Star trackers are widely considered the most accurate sensors for this task, as they provide absolute orientation by observing distant stellar constellations. However, the classical ap-proach to star identification (Star-ID) typically involves a two-stage process: star centroiding followed by sub-graph matching against a massive onboard catalog. Al-gorithms such as Pyramid [1] and Grid [2] are mathe-matically rigorous but struggle with the ”Lost-in-Space” scenario when faced with high-dynamic maneuvers, sig-nificant sensor noise, or ”false stars” from space debris. 

1.1 Motivation and Problem Statement 

The rise of Deep Representation Learning offers a promis-ing alternative to classical Star-ID. By treating orientation as a vision task, models can, in principle, learn robust features invariant to noise. Nevertheless, existing deep learning models face a significant hurdle: the spherical wrap-around problem . Because RA ranges from 0◦ to 

360 ◦, two spatially adjacent orientations can be numeri-cally distant in the coordinate space. Direct regression of these values often leads to unstable training and high error rates near the 0/360 boundary. Furthermore, vanilla Vision Transformers (ViTs) often lack an explicit ”geometric prior.” While they excel at cap-turing global textures, they do not inherently respect the point-wise geometric relationships that define a star con-stellation. There is a clear need for a multi-modal archi-1

1.2 Contributions 

In this work, we propose the Star-Fusion framework to bridge the gap between vision-based pattern recognition and geometric celestial mechanics. Our primary contri-butions are as follows: • Spherical Discretization Strategy: We introduce a labeling method based on spherical K-Means clus-tering. 5.5 Error Analysis 

An analysis of the confusion matrix reveals that 62% of misclassifications occurred between adjacent clusters at the celestial equator. This suggests that while the model is robust, the quantization error at the K = 12 bound-ary is the primary source of inaccuracy. This justifies future research into overlapping cluster memberships or soft-labeling. 

# 6 Discussion 

The performance gains observed in the Star-Fusion ar-chitecture provide compelling evidence for the necessity of multi-modal integration in celestial navigation. Our analysis indicates that the three processing branches play complementary roles in the feature extraction process. 

6.1 Synergistic Feature Extraction 

The Photometric Branch (SwinV2) demonstrates aunique ability to capture global contextual cues, such as stellar density gradients and unresolved galactic back-ground noise, which provide a ”visual signature” for spe-cific celestial regions. However, transformers can occa-sionally be indifferent to precise pixel-level shifts. This is mitigated by the Geometric Heatmap Branch ,which enforces spatial grounding. By representing stars as Gaussian kernels, we translate sparse point data into a dense spatial manifold that the CNN can effectively pro-cess. Finally, the Coordinate MLP acts as a numerical anchor, providing the model with high-fidelity geomet-ric ratios that are invariant to photometric fluctuations. The fusion of these disparate data types allows the model to maintain high confidence even when one modality is degraded for instance, during high-exposure ”blooming” events where photometric details are lost but centroids re-main detectable. 

6.2 The Discretization Trade-off 

The transition from continuous regression to discrete clas-sification via spherical K-Means clustering represents a significant departure from traditional deep-learning-based star trackers. This approach effectively ”smoothes” the loss landscape, allowing the model to converge on a global orientation without the instability typically associ-ated with angular wrap-around. However, we observe that the model’s confidence scores tend to dip near the Voronoi boundaries of the clusters. This suggests that the model is internalizing the geometric proximity of adjacent classes, a property we intend to exploit in future work through soft-labeling and manifold learning. 

# 7 Limitations 

Despite the robust performance of Star-Fusion, several limitations must be addressed before deployment in a production Attitude Determination and Control System (ADCS). • Quantization Error: With K = 12 orientation classes, each class represents a significantly large solid angle of the celestial sphere. While this is suf-ficient for a ”Lost-in-Space” coarse acquisition, it does not currently provide the arc-second precision required for fine-pointing maneuvers (e.g., telescope stabilization). • Sensor-Specific Biases: The current model is trained primarily on synthetic data derived from the 

Hipparcos catalog. Real-world sensor effects, such as non-uniform dark current, lens distortion, and 4radiation-induced ”hot pixels,” may introduce a do-main gap. While data augmentation (Gaussian noise, magnitude clipping) was employed, on-orbit perfor-mance may vary. • High-Dynamic Scenarios: In scenarios involving high angular velocities ( > 5◦/s ), motion blur can cause stars to appear as streaks. While the Coordi-nate MLP is theoretically more robust to such defor-mations, the SwinV2 backbone’s performance under extreme motion-blur kernels remains a subject for further empirical validation. 

# 8 Future Work 

Future iterations of this research will explore Hierar-chical Spherical Clustering , where a primary classifier identifies a coarse region, and a secondary specialized head performs fine-grained regression. Additionally, we aim to implement Temporal Attention across consecu-tive frames to utilize the satellite’s trajectory as a prior for attitude propagation. 

8.1 Computational Complexity 

The total parameter count of Star-Fusion is approxi-mately 29.4M. On an NVIDIA Jetson Orin (simulating spacecraft hardware), the inference latency is ∼12ms per image, satisfying real-time navigation requirements for high-dynamic maneuvers. 

# 9 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced Star-Fusion , a novel multi-modal transformer architecture designed to address the topological and computational challenges of spacecraft attitude determination. By reformulating orientation es-timation as a discrete classification task over a clustered celestial sphere, we successfully mitigated the periodic discontinuities inherent in angular coordinate regression. Our experimental results demonstrate that the tripartite fusion of photometric (SwinV2), spatial (CNN Heatmap), and numerical (Coordinate MLP) features yields a robust representational capacity. The model achieved a Top-1 accuracy of 93.4%, representing a 9.2% improvement over unimodal vision-based baselines. Furthermore, with an inference latency of 18.4 ms on embedded hardware, Star-Fusion satisfies the stringent real-time constraints re-quired for high-dynamic satellite maneuvers. The implications of this research are particularly rel-evant for the ”New Space” industry, where resource-constrained platforms such as CubeSats require efficient, ”Lost-in-Space” robust navigation systems that can oper-ate without heavy database overhead. By offloading the geometric complexity to a pre-trained multi-modal net-work, we provide a scalable framework for autonomous celestial navigation. 

9.1 Future Directions 

While this study establishes a robust foundation for dis-crete orientation, several avenues for future research re-main. 1. Coarse-to-Fine Refinement: We intend to imple-ment a recursive regression head that utilizes the pre-dicted class as a spatial prior to achieve sub-degree angular precision. 2. Temporal Consistency: Leveraging Recurrent Neu-ral Networks (RNNs) or Temporal Transformers could allow the model to utilize the satellite’s orbital mechanics as a temporal constraint, further smooth-ing orientation transitions. 3. On-Orbit Domain Adaptation: Future work will explore self-supervised fine-tuning techniques to adapt the model to specific sensor noise profiles di-rectly in the space environment, ensuring long-term mission reliability.
