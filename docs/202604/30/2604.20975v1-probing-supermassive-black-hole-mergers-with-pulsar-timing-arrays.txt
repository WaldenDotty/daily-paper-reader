## Probing Supermassive Black Hole Mergers with Pulsar Timing Arrays 

Hippolyte Quelquejay Leclere ∗

Dipartimento di Fisica “G. Occhialini”, Universit` a degli Studi di Milano-Bicocca, Piazza della Scienza 3, I-20126 Milano, Italy. 

(Dated: April 24, 2026) By monitoring the times of arrival of radio pulses from millisecond pulsars, Pulsar Timing Arrays (PTAs) serve as unique gravitational wave (GW) laboratories in the nanohertz band. To date, the primary astrophysical sources of GWs targeted in this frequency range have been inspiraling supermassive black hole binaries (SMBHBs) on circular and eccentric orbits. In this work, we demonstrate that, thanks to the so-called pulsar term in the timing residual waveform of GW signals, PTAs can probe individual SMBHBs that merged before timing observations began. We refer to the latter as zombie binaries . Using SMBHB population models consistent with current PTA constraints, we find that while the probability of detecting such systems in existing PTA datasets remains low, the Square Kilometer Array observatory is expected to achieve sufficient sensitivity to have a few zombie binaries with a signal-to-noise ratio exceeding 3 in its data. Although their confident identification might be challenging, this new class of PTA sources opens a novel window for studying the most massive SMBHBs in our local universe. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Recently, Pulsar Timing Array (PTA) collaborations have reported compelling evidence for the presence of Gravitational Wave (GW) signals in their datasets [1–5]. The primary source of these nanohertz GWs is believed to be the population of inspiraling Supermassive Black Hole Binaries (SMBHBs) [6, 7], which are expected to form within the hierarchical model of galaxy formation [8, 9]. If supermassive black hole pairs manage to efficiently reach sub-parsec separation within a Hubble time [10] – through dynamical friction and stellar hardening [11, 12] – they become efficient sources of GWs in the 1 − 100 nanohertz band, until they merge at orbital frequencies of hundreds of nanohertz. Therefore, binaries emitting in the PTA band are generally thousands to millions of years from merger and are thus expected to be observed as quasi-monochromatic sources over PTAs timescale. Up to now, PTAs have searched for SMBHBs using two main approaches, although alternative methods are also possible and used [13–16]. The first involves detecting the stochastic GW background produced by the population of binaries, which is expected to imprint a quadrupo-lar correlation pattern among pulsars’ timing residuals, known as the Hellings-Downs curve [17]. The presence of such a signal has been reported with strong evidence by various PTAs, along with a characterization of its power spectral density in the 1 − 10 nanohertz range, mostly consistent across collaborations [18]. Although poten-tially degenerate with, for example, other cosmological GW backgrounds [19, 20], the identification of signifi-cant anisotropies [21, 22], non-stationarities [23] or non-Gaussianities [24, 25] could help proving the astrophys-ical origin of the stochastic signal. But collaborations

have not yet found strong evidence of such features [26– 29]. The second, more direct approach is the search for GW signals from individual binaries, inspiraling on circu-lar or eccentric orbits, in the PTA data [30–32]. The pul-sars’ timing residuals induced by an inspiraling SMBHB are made of two terms, respectively produced by the met-ric perturbation at the pulse emissions (pulsar term) and their reception (Earth term). So far, no conclusive evi-dence for the presence of such a system has been reported [33, 34]. Pitkin [35] was the first to notice that the signal from an individual SMBHB could take a distinct form in the case of a SMBHB merging in the millihertz band of the Laser Interferometer Space Antenna (LISA) detector. In this scenario, the pulsar terms associated with this binary could still fall within the PTA band, thereby enabling the possibility of multi-band observations of SMBHBs [36]. In this work, we extend beyond the case of binaries merg-ing at present and show that PTAs can also detect past mergers of SMBHB, due to the kiloparsec-scale distances between the monitored pulsars and Earth. Hereafter, we refer to this new class of PTA sources as zombie bina-ries . The paper is organized as follows. In Section II, we first explain the principles of zombie binary detection with PTAs. We then present how to estimate the like-lihood of observing zombie binaries with high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) in PTA datasets in Section III. This depends both on the SMBHB population models consid-ered, which we present in Section IV, and on the PTA sensitivity to zombie binary signals, which we estimate in Section V. We then present our results for past, cur-rent, and future PTA sensitivities in Section VI, before discussing the implications of our findings in Section VII. In the following, we define zombie binaries as binaries whose Earth term orbital frequency at t0, f (E) orb , exceeds a thresh-old frequency, fth . We note that this includes the case of binaries that merged before t0, for which we consider that f (E) orb = + ∞. In this paper, we use fth = 3 ×10 −7 Hz, a frequency at the edge of the PTA band. > VI. RESULTS

In order to quantify the capacity of PTAs to probe zombie binaries, we consider representative configura-tions for past, current and future PTA datasets, which are summarized in Table II. They aim to represent re-spectively the sensitivity of the European PTA (EPTA) second data release (referred to as DR2new in [55]), the upcoming International PTA (IPTA) third data release (DR3) and a potential future sensitivity of the Square Kilometer Array (SKA) observatory [56]. For the IPTA DR3-like sensitivity, we consider 130 pulsars present in the previously published collaboration datasets, fixing their observing time to 10 years, with an observing ca-dence of 5 days. For the SKA configuration, we use 124 pulsars of our IPTA-like dataset with declination angle below 45 ◦ [57] and added randomly 6 pulsars from [58]. For pulsars whose distances to Earth could not be esti-mated, we randomly assign them a distance from 0 .5 to 2.5 kiloparsecs using uniform weighting. In Figure 3, we show the detection efficiency for zom-bie binaries, evaluated for each PTA configuration. We find that the EPTA DR2new-like dataset can only probe massive systems with Mc,r ≈ 10 9M⊙ at redshift below 0.03. However, such PTAs could still efficiently probe the very massive – but rare – mergers with Mc,r ≈ 10 10 M⊙

up to z ≈ 1. Since the SNRs of zombie binaries scale as pNpsr , the situation improves only slightly for our IPTA DR3 setting. However, the true IPTA DR3 dataset will be much more heterogeneous with some pulsars hav-ing an observing time longer than 10 years with a bet-ter cadence, as well as lower timing precision [18]. This could significantly enhanced the detection efficiency of this dataset and we therefore regard our IPTA sensitiv-ity as a conservative baseline. Going to the SKA con-figuration, we find that zombie binaries of a few billion solar masses (or above) could be efficiently probed up to redshift 1. Less massive mergers with Mc,r < 10 9M⊙

could also be detected providing they happened at very low redshift, typically at z ≲ 0.1. Using our numerical detection efficiencies, we then evaluate Eq. 10 for each of the three SMBHBs population models presented in Section IV. Our results are summa-rized in Figure 4, where we show the probability to have at least one zombie binary with SNR above 3 for each PTA-population model pair. While EPTA-like datasets are not likely to be able to detect any zombie binary – P ( Nz > 0) ≤ 3% – the situation strongly improves for IPTA-like and SKA-like datasets. Although the IPTA configuration has a very small chance to detect zombie binaries under M1 – due to its limited detection efficiency for systems with Mc,r < 10 9M⊙ – the probability in-creases to 23% and 22% for M2 and M3, respectively. Notably, for the SKA-like PTA, P ( Nz > 0) ≥ 90%, for all three population models. We find that such dataset should be sensitive to, on average, 2 .3, 6 .1 and 5 .7 zombie binaries considering the M1, M2 and M3 models respec-tively. Even in a far more pessimistic scenario where 

σMN = 300 ns for all SKA pulsars, ⟨Nz⟩ remains around 1 for both the M2 and M3 models – although it drops to 0 .06 for M1. It thus appears very likely that, if the spatially-correlated signal seen by PTA collaborations is indeed sourced by a population of SMBHBs, the future SKA facility will be able to detect at least one past binary merger. This sweet spot where zombie binaries are found allows around 50% of bright systems to reach SNR > 5, for the three population models considered. 

VII. DISCUSSION 

In this work, we demonstrated that PTAs have the ca-pacity of probing the mergers of SMBHBs that occurred before the start of the experiment. This new class of PTA sources – which we call zombie binaries – are accessible thanks to the time delays of several thousands of years that separate the so-called pulsar term from the Earth term in the timing residuals induced by a GW source. Considering the case of circular binaries driven by GW emission, we estimated the number of zombie binaries with a SNR greater than 3 that past (EPTA DR2), cur-rent (IPTA DR3) and future (SKA) PTA datasets are likely to be sensitive to. In order to capture the current uncertainty on the merger rate of SMBHBs, we consid-ered three analytic comoving merger density models – based on Schechter-like functions – compatible with cur-rent PTA observations. While EPTA DR2-like datasets lack sensitivity to yield a significant probability of detect-ing zombie binaries, we find that, for SMBHBs models favoring large mass systems (with Mc,r > 10 9M⊙), the IPTA DR3 dataset has a 23% probability of containing at least one zombie binary with SNR > 3. For the SKA sen-sitivity considered here, we find that, for all population models, this probability is greater than 90%. We predict that a few bright zombie binaries are likely to be detected 7in future SKA datasets if the currently observed PTA signal is sourced by a population of SMBHBs. These massive mergers ( Mc,r > 10 9M⊙) associated with pul-sar term signals’ frequency on the order of 10 nanohertz, will typically be probed at low redshift ( z ≲ 1). However, we note that these findings rely on the assumption that SMBHBs are on circular orbits. If the signal observed by PTAs originates from a population of binaries that are on very eccentric orbits when entering the PTA band – as recent studies suggest [59, 60] – then zombie bina-ries may still have significant eccentricities. Because the orbital evolution of eccentric binaries is faster, zombie binaries could be found at lower orbital frequencies than in the circular case. This should be explored in future work. We also note that the SNR calculations used in this work do not account for our current uncertainty about the pulsar distances and therefore could be considered optimistic. However, precise distance measurements us-ing very long baseline interferometry is becoming increas-ingly common for millisecond pulsars [61]. In addition, the significant improvement in timing precision allowed by the SKA is expected to enable pulsar distance mea-surements with less than 5% error for the pulsars’ dis-tances considered in this work [62]. Achieving a distance accuracy for pulsars on the order of hundreds of light-years will allow precise determination of the frequencies of the pulsar terms. This will help the coherent recon-struction of zombie binary signals across multiple pulsars, which is required for a future robust detection. The ac-tual detectability of these systems in realistic simulated PTA datasets is deferred to future work. Such indirect detection of binary mergers would open a remarkable window on the study of the environment and dynamics of SMBHBs during the last thousands of years before coalescence. However, it will also represent a challenge for detecting their signals, since the wave-form associated with each pulsar of the array depends on the orbital frequency evolution model considered. In any case, the search for zombie binaries will – and could already – put new constraints on the high-mass end of SMBHBs merger rate models. This will be investigated in future work.
