TOPOLOGICAL DETECTION OF HOPF BIFURCATIONS VIA PERSISTENT HOMOLOGY: A FUNCTIONAL CRITERION FROM TIME SERIES 

JHONATHAN BARRIOS, YÁSSER ECHÁVEZ, AND CARLOS F. ÁLVAREZ 

Abstract. We propose a topological framework for the detection of Hopf bifurcations directly from time series, based on persistent homology applied to phase space recon-structions via Takens embedding within the framework of Topological Data Analysis (TDA). The central idea is that changes in the dynamical regime are reflected in the emergence or disappearance of a dominant one-dimensional homological features in the reconstructed attractor. To quantify this behavior, we introduce a simple and inter-pretable scalar topological functional defined as the maximum persistence of homology classes in dimension one. This functional is used to construct a computable criterion for identifying critical parameters in families of dynamical systems without requiring knowl-edge of the underlying equations. The proposed approach is validated on representative systems of increasing complexity, showing consistent detection of the bifurcation point. The results support the interpretation of dynamical transitions as topological phase tran-sitions and demonstrate the potential of topological data analysis as a model-free tool for the quantitative analysis of nonlinear time series. 

1. Introduction 

Bifurcations constitute one of the fundamental mechanisms through which nonlinear dy-namic systems experience qualitative changes in their behavior [12]. Among them, the Hopf bifurcation occupies a central place, as it describes the transition from a stable equilibrium to a periodic oscillatory regime through the emergence of a limit cycle [12, 23, 26]. This phenomenon is of particular interest in a wide variety of applied contexts, as it allows us to explain the emergence of self-sustained oscillations in physical, biological, chemical, and engineering systems. Representative examples include models of circadian rhythms, neu-ronal dynamics, oscillatory chemical reactions, and stability problems in control systems and electrical networks [10, 14]. Given the interest in this phenomenon, the detection of Hopf bifurcations is important because it allows for the identification of critical transitions in a dynamical system before they fully manifest in its observable behavior [25]. From an applied perspective, recognizing the transition from a steady-state to an oscillatory regime can be crucial for understanding, predicting, or controlling the system’s evolution. In par-ticular, the emergence of periodic oscillations can be associated with functional behaviors, as in certain biological processes, or with the loss of desired stability, as in engineering problems or the appearance of pathological dynamics in neural models. [17, 26]. The study of Hopf bifurcations has relied on classical tools for the analysis of dynamical systems, such as the study of eigenvalues of the linearized Jacobian, the construction of bifurcation diagrams, phase portraits, and the calculation of Lyapunov exponents [1, 17, 23]. However, these methods often require explicit knowledge of the equations governing the system or, at least, access to sufficient structural information to carry out a local or global analysis of the dynamics. This dependence on the model represents a significant limitation in real-world problems, where frequently only observed time series are available and not a precise mathematical description of the underlying system. difficulty is the fact that many systems of practical interest generate noisy, incomplete, or highly complex data, situations in which traditional methods can lose precision, robustness, or interpretability. For example, the estimation of Lyapunov exponents can be sensitive to series length and measurement noise, while the visual identification of changes in phase portraits or bifurcation diagrams does not always produce easily reproducible quantitative criteria. Consequently, the need arises to develop alternative, data-driven approaches that reliably detect dynamic transitions such as Hopf bifurcations [3]. In this context, Takens’ theorem provides a key mathematical foundation for the study of dynamical systems using time series. This result establishes that, under general hy-potheses, it is possible to reconstruct a representation of the underlying attractor using delay embeddings from a single scalar time series [27]. This idea opens the door to studying geometric and topological properties of dynamics even when the explicit model is unknown [24, 27]. In recent years, Topological Data Analysis (TDA) has emerged as a powerful frame-work for extracting structural information from complex data. In particular, persistent homology allows for the identification of robust topological features at multiple scales, such as connected components, cycles, and cavities, and distinguishes between meaningful structures and artifacts due to noise [4, 6, 8]. These properties make TDA a particularly attractive tool for reconstructed time series analysis, where the global geometry of the at-tractor (a compact invariant set toward which nearby orbits converge) can contain relevant information about qualitative changes in the dynamics [22]. In fact, recent work has shown that TDA can be used to study dynamical transitions and detect bifurcations in nonlinear systems. These include approaches based on CROCKER plots [11], zigzag persistent homology [30], and other topological representations of re-constructed attractors [19, 28]. While these methods provide valuable qualitative insights into the evolution of topological structures, they typically rely on visual analysis or high-dimensional summaries, which may limit their direct use as quantitative detection tools. Consequently, there remains a need for explicit, interpretable, and computable topological criteria that enable the systematic detection of bifurcations directly from time series data. Motivated by this need, in this paper we propose a topological characterization of the Hopf bifurcation based on persistent homology applied to attractors reconstructed by de-lays. The central idea is that the transition from a stable equilibrium to a limit cycle induces the emergence of a dominant cyclic structure in the reconstructed point cloud. From a topological perspective, this transition manifests as the appearance of a persistent one-dimensional homological class that is well separated from the diagonal in the persis-tence diagram. Based on this observation, we introduce a scalar topological functional that quantifies the prominence of such cyclic structures and propose a computable criterion for estimating the critical bifurcation parameter along a parametric family of time series. To evaluate the scope of the proposed approach, the criterion is numerically validated on representative dynamical systems of different natures. In particular, the Hopf normal form is considered as a canonical case with a known critical value, the Lorenz system as an exam-ple of more complex nonlinear dynamics, and a reduced model of the Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction as an additional test of generality. This validation strategy allows us to assess both the ability of the method to identify the transition between equilibrium and oscilla-tory behavior and its robustness across systems with different geometrical and dynamical complexity. The remainder of the article is organized as follows. In the Section 2 , the basic mathe-matical elements of Hopf bifurcations, phase space reconstruction, and persistent homology are presented. In the Section 3, the proposed topological characterization and detection criterion are introduced. Section 4 describes the computational methodology used to im-plement the approach. In the Section 5, the numerical experiments and validation on the considered systems are presented. Finally, in 6, the results are discussed, and the conclusions of the work are presented. This section presents the mathematical concepts necessary for developing the proposed topological criterion: 2.1. Hopf bifurcations. Although the notion of dynamical system is broad in mathe-matics, throughout this work we restrict attention to dynamical systems generated by the solution (flow) of a system of ordinary differential equations. A bifurcation of a dynamical system is a qualitative change in its dynamics produced by varying a parameter. Consider a one-parameter family of smooth dynamical systems 

˙x = f (x, μ ), x ∈ Rn, μ ∈ I ⊂ R, (1) where μ is a control parameter. An equilibrium point x∗ satisfies f (x∗, μ ) = 0 . Recall that the phase space of system (1) is Rn, the space of all possible states x. For fixed μ0 ∈ I, a closed orbit Γμ0 is called a limit cycle if there exists ε > 0 such that no other closed orbit lies within distance ε of Γμ0 .A Hopf bifurcation occurs when a pair of complex conjugate eigenvalues λ(μ) of the Jacobian Df (x∗, μ ) crosses the imaginary axis as μ varies. Under suitable non-degeneracy conditions, this transition leads to the emergence of a limit cycle in a neighborhood of the equilibrium [17, 26]. More precisely, there exists a critical value μc such that 

Re( λ(μc)) = 0 .

For parameter values near μc, the system transitions between a stable equilibrium and periodic dynamics. This phenomenon describes the emergence of self-sustained oscillations in numerous physical, biological, and chemical systems. 2.2. Phase space reconstruction. In many applications the underlying dynamical sys-tem is not explicitly known and only a scalar time series is observed. Examples arise in gait dynamics, biomechanical sensors, and financial time series, where the system must be inferred from data [2, 9]. Here, an attractor is a compact invariant set toward which all nearby orbits converge asymptotically. Takens’ theorem states that, under generic condi-tions, the geometry of the underlying attractor can be reconstructed by means of a delay embedding constructed from the observed time series [27]. Let x(t) be a scalar time series. The delay embedding is the map 

Φ( t) =  x(t), x (t − τ ), x (t − 2τ ), . . . , x (t − (m − 1) τ ) ∈ Rm, (2) where τ > 0 is the time delay and m ≥ 1 is the embedding dimension. Under generic conditions, Φ defines an embedding of the original attractor into Rm that preserves its topological and geometric properties [27]; this result was later extended to attractors of fractal dimension in [24]. The resulting point cloud 

X = {Φ( tk)}Nk=1 ⊂ Rm (3) thus constitutes a reconstruction of the attractor from the observed time series. 2.3. Persistent homology. Topological Data Analysis provides tools for studying global properties of datasets via topological invariants. Among these, persistent homology is one of the most widely used techniques for identifying robust topological structures in data [6, 8]. Recall that a simplicial complex is a finite collection of simplices (vertices, edges, tri-angles, and their higher-dimensional analogues) closed under taking faces. Given a finite point cloud X ⊂ Rm and a scale parameter ε ≥ 0, the Vietoris–Rips complex VR( X, ε ) is the simplicial complex whose simplices are subsets of X with pairwise distances less than 

ε. Varying ε yields a nested family {VR( X, ε )}ε≥0, called a filtration .4

Following Hatcher [13, Chapter 2], the first homology group H1 of a topological space is the abelianization of its fundamental group. Informally, a non-trivial class in H1 cor-responds to a loop that cannot be continuously contracted to a point it detects a one-dimensional “hole”. In the context of a point cloud, such a class signals a cyclic structure that persists across a range of scales ε.Persistent homology tracks the birth and death of such homology classes as ε varies across the filtration. The output is a persistence diagram 

D = {(bi, d i)},

where bi and di denote, respectively, the birth and death scale of the i-th homological feature, and the quantity di − bi measures its persistence . Applied to dynamical systems, non-trivial classes in H1 are typically associated with periodic orbits or limit cycles in the reconstructed attractor [21]. This makes persistent homology a natural tool for quantifying geometry in point clouds derived from time series and for detecting dynamical transitions. In terms of implementation, from persistent homology we have the persistence diagrams that provide a compact representation of the birth and death of topological features across scales. Each point (b, d ) in the diagram corresponds to a homological feature that appears at scale b and disappears at scale d, with its persistence given by d − b [31]. 3. Hopf’s Topological Criterion 

The emergence of a limit cycle after a Hopf bifurcation implies a qualitative change in the geometry of the system’s attractor. Before the bifurcation, the trajectories converge toward an equilibrium point, resulting in a point cloud concentrated in a small region of the reconstructed space. After the bifurcation, the dynamics evolve toward a periodic orbit, generating an approximately circular structure in phase space. From a topological perspective, this transition corresponds to the emergence of a nontrivial homology class in dimension one [8, 21]. While no significant persistent cycles are observed before the bifurcation, a dominant class in H1 appears afterward, associated with the presence of the limit cycle. This observation suggests that the Hopf transition can be detected through the emergence of a persistent dominant class in H1. Computational Implementation 

The computational implementation of the proposed topological criterion follows a pipeline that integrates time series generation, phase space reconstruction, and topological analysis via persistent homology. All experiments were implemented in Python using specialized libraries for dynamical systems simulation and topological data analysis. Starting from observed or simulated time series, the phase space is reconstructed via delay embedding. Persistent homology is then computed on the resulting point cloud, and the proposed topological functional (Section 3) is evaluated to detect qualitative changes in the system’s dynamics. 4.1. Time Series Generation. Time series from classical dynamical systems exhibiting Hopf bifurcations were used. The systems analyzed included the Hopf normal form, the Lorenz system and the reduced model of the Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction, widely used as reference models in the study of nonlinear dynamics. The time series were generated by numerical integration of the differential equations using the fourth-order Runge-Kutta method. The simulation was implemented in Python using tools from the Teaspoon li-brary, a package designed for the topological processing of signals and dynamical systems [16]. This environment provides efficient implementations for generating trajectories of dynamical systems and facilitates their integration with topological analysis tools. In each experiment, a scalar variable of the dynamical system, denoted by x(t), was selected, from which the attractor reconstruction was performed. HOPF’S TOPOLOGICAL CRITERION 7

4.2. Implementation of the Topological Criterion. Since in many applications the underlying dynamical system is not explicitly known and only an observed scalar time series is available, Takens’ theorem was used to reconstruct the phase space from a delay embedding [27]. The embedding parameters were determined using classical criteria from nonlinear time series analysis, such as mutual information for estimating the time delay [7] and the nearest false neighbors method for estimating the embedding dimension [15]. This reconstruction was implemented using the SingleTakensEmbedding class from the 

giotto-tda library [29]. The reconstructed point cloud is analyzed using persistent homology, focusing on di-mension one ( H1), which captures cyclic structures associated with oscillatory behavior. Persistence diagrams are computed using Vietoris–Rips filtrations based on pairwise dis-tances between embedded points. The computation was performed using efficient imple-mentations available in the ripser and giotto-tda libraries, which allow the extraction of persistence diagrams across parameter values. Finally, once the persistence diagrams are obtained for each parameter value, the pro-posed topological functional is evaluated. This functional quantifies the prominence of the dominant one-dimensional homological feature and serves as the core indicator of struc-tural changes in the reconstructed attractor. The evolution of H (μ) along the parameter allows for the detection of qualitative transitions in the system, which are interpreted as estimates of the bifurcation point. 4.3. Computational Complexity. Let N be the number of points in each reconstructed time series and M be the number of parameter values analyzed. The cost of reconstruction using Takens embedding is O(N ) per time series. The calculation of persistent homology using Vietoris-Rips complexes is the computationally dominant step of the method. In practice, modern implementations such as ripser use optimizations that allow the low-dimensional homology to be calculated with a complexity of approximately O(N 2) for moderate point clouds. Since the procedure is applied to M parameter values, the total cost of the algorithm can be approximated by O(M N 2). In typical dynamic systems analysis applications, where the number of embedding points is kept within a moderate 8

range, this cost is manageable and allows for efficient analysis of the system’s topological evolution along the control parameter. Further implementation details and methodological considerations are provided in Ap-pendix B. 5. Experiments and Results 

In this section, we evaluate the performance of the proposed topological criterion on three dynamical systems of increasing complexity, denoted as cases A, B, and C. The ob-jective is to evaluate the ability of the method to detect Hopf-type transitions directly from observed time series. First, we considered the normal form of the Hopf bifurcation, which serves as a reference case with a known analytical critical parameter. This allows us to validate the accuracy of the proposed topological estimator under controlled conditions. Next, the criterion is applied to the Lorenz system, providing a more challenging scenario characterized by higher dimensionality and complex nonlinear dynamics. This case en-ables the evaluation of the robustness of the method beyond simple bifurcation settings. Finally, we analyze a reduced model of the Belousov–Zhabotinsky (BZ) reaction, a classical chemical system exhibiting oscillatory behavior and Hopf-type transitions. In this case, the transition corresponds to the disappearance of a coherent oscillatory structure, offer-ing a complementary scenario to the previous experiments and allowing us to assess the generality of the proposed approach. For completeness, we also compare the behavior of the proposed topological functional with classical dynamical indicators, such as the largest Lyapunov exponent, to further support the interpretation of the detected transitions. 

Case A: Normal form of Hopf bifurcation. As a first experiment for the validation of the proposed topological criterion, the normal form of the supercritical Hopf bifurcation was considered, given by the system: 

˙x = μx − ωy − x(x2 + y2), ˙y = ωx + μy − y(x2 + y2); 

where μ is the control parameter and ω > 0 represents the angular frequency of the system. This system exhibits a Hopf bifurcation at the critical value μc = 0 . For μ < 0,the origin is a stable equilibrium, while for μ > 0, a stable limit cycle emerges. This example allows us to evaluate, in a controlled environment, whether the criterion based on persistent homology is capable of correctly detecting the transition between a stationary regime and an oscillatory regime from time series. For different values of the parameter μ ∈ [−1, 1] , time series of the system were generated and the phase space was reconstructed using Takens embedding with delay τ = 26 and embedding dimension m = 2 . From the reconstructed point cloud, persistent homology was computed and the topological functional H (μ) defined in Section 3 was evaluated. 

Results. Figure 1 shows the persistence diagrams obtained for different values of μ, which exhibit a sharp topological transition consistent with the theory. For μ < 0, the diagrams are dominated by homology classes H0 and low-persistence features H1, concentrated in the vicinity of the birth-death diagonal. Qualitatively, this indicates the absence of robust cyclic structures in the reconstructed phase space, consistent with a dynamics dominated by a stable equilibrium point. In contrast, as μ approaches the critical neighborhood, a class H1 emerges, clearly separated from the diagonal, constituting the first topological evidence of the appearance of a periodic orbit. For μ > 0, this persistent class consolidates and its lifetime progressively increases, reflecting the formation and stabilization of the limit cycle. From the perspective of the topological criterion, this result is fundamental: the bifurcation manifests as the abrupt emergence of a dominant homological feature in one dimension. In other words, the transition from stable equilibrium to periodic dynamics is encoded as a qualitative change in the topological structure of the reconstructed attractor, which directly supports the validity of the proposed criterion. Summary of findings. In this work, we proposed a topological framework for detect-ing Hopf-type transitions directly from time series data using persistent homology applied to phase space reconstructions. The central hypothesis—that such transitions are asso-ciated with the emergence or disappearance of a dominant one-dimensional topological feature—was validated across three dynamical systems of increasing complexity. The pro-posed functional proved to be an effective and interpretable descriptor of these transitions. In the Hopf normal form, it accurately identifies the critical parameter associated with the onset of a limit cycle. In the Lorenz system, the functional exhibits robustness under noise and captures structural changes in a chaotic regime. In the BZ reaction, the method suc-cessfully detects a transition characterized by the loss of a coherent oscillatory structure, demonstrating its ability to identify qualitatively different dynamical scenarios. Furthermore, the analysis of the relationship between the proposed functional and the largest Lyapunov exponent revealed strong correlations in all cases, particularly in the Lorenz system. At the same time, the weaker correlation observed in the BZ system highlights that the topological functional captures structural features of the dynamics that are not necessarily reflected in classical stability indicators. 

Impact and relevance. These results support the interpretation of Hopf-type transitions as topological phase transitions, characterized by changes in the persistence of homological features. From a methodological perspective, the proposed approach provides a model-free and data-driven framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, complementing classical techniques based on local stability. In contrast to existing TDA-based approaches, which often rely on descriptive summaries of persistence diagrams or qualitative compar-isons across parameter regimes, the present work introduces a simple scalar functional that enables a direct and quantitative criterion for detecting dynamical transitions. In particular, the ability to identify both the emergence and disappearance of coherent cyclic structures directly from time series data represents a significant advantage in applications where the governing equations are unknown or difficult to estimate. This positions TDA not only as a descriptive tool, but as a practical framework for quantitative inference in dy-namical systems, capable of revealing geometric information that is not accessible through traditional indicators. 16 

Limitations. Despite its advantages, the proposed approach has some limitations. First, the method relies on phase space reconstruction via Takens embedding, which requires the selection of embedding parameters such as the delay and embedding dimension. While standard techniques exist, the choice of these parameters can influence the resulting topo-logical descriptors. Second, the computation of persistent homology, particularly through Vietoris–Rips complexes, can become computationally demanding for large datasets. Al-though the present study focuses on moderate time series lengths and low-dimensional homology, scalability remains an important consideration. Finally, the analysis was re-stricted to synthetic systems with controlled dynamics. While this allows for clear val-idation, further work is required to assess the performance of the method in real-world settings. 

Future directions. Several directions for future research can emerge from this work. A natural extension is the application of the proposed framework to other types of bifurca-tions, such as period-doubling bifurcations, where different topological features may arise, or in the study of limit cycles in complex dynamical systems such as Lienard-type systems. Another important direction is the development of systematic criteria for the selection of embedding parameters and their impact on topological features, potentially leading to more robust and theoretically grounded methodologies. From a statistical perspective, incorporating uncertainty quantification and hypothesis testing based on topological func-tionals represents a promising avenue for advancing the data-driven analysis of dynamical systems. Finally, the application of the proposed method to real-world time series, par-ticularly in biological, chemical, or physiological systems, constitutes a key step toward demonstrating its practical relevance and broader applicability.

These results highlight that while Lyapunov exponents provide valuable information about local stability and chaos, they do not fully capture the global structural changes in the dynamics. This motivates the use of topological methods, which are able to detect both the emergence and disappearance of coherent structures directly from time series data.

Phase space reconstruction. The analysis relies on phase space reconstruction via Tak-ens embedding. For each system, the embedding parameters, namely the time delay τ and the embedding dimension m, were estimated using standard techniques from nonlinear time series analysis, including mutual information for delay selection and the false nearest neighbors method for dimension estimation. While the choice of embedding parameters can influence the resulting topological features, the objective of this work is not to opti-mize embedding strategies but to evaluate the behavior of the proposed functional under consistent reconstruction settings. To evaluate the robustness of the method, additional 18 

experiments were conducted by varying the embedding parameters around the estimated values. These results show that, although the absolute magnitude of the topological func-tional may vary slightly, its qualitative behavior and the detection of the transition remain stable under reasonable changes in τ and m.A systematic study of embedding parameter selection and its interaction with topological descriptors remains an important direction for future research. 

Persistent homology computation. Persistent homology was computed using Vietoris– Rips complexes constructed from the Euclidean distances between points in the recon-structed phase space. The analysis was restricted to homology in dimension one, which is sufficient to capture cyclic structures associated with oscillatory behavior while keeping the computational cost manageable. 

Computation Cost. The proposed methodology involves several computational steps, including time series generation, phase space reconstruction via Takens embedding, and the computation of persistent homology from the resulting point clouds. Among these steps, the most computationally demanding component is the construction of the Vietoris–Rips complex and the computation of persistence diagrams. Let N denote the number of points in the reconstructed phase space. While the worst-case complexity of Vietoris–Rips persistence is exponential in N , in practice the computa-tional burden is significantly reduced by restricting the analysis to low homological dimen-sions and using efficient implementations. In this work, persistent homology is computed only in dimension one, which makes the approach tractable for time series of moderate length. The Takens embedding introduces an additional cost of order O(N ), while the evaluation of the dominant topological functional H is computationally negligible once the persistence diagram is obtained. In our experiments, the overall computation time remained within practical limits for all systems considered. It is important to note that the proposed approach can be computationally more de-manding than classical methods such as Lyapunov exponent estimation. However, the two methodologies address fundamentally different aspects of the dynamics. Lyapunov expo-nents provide information about local stability and sensitivity to initial conditions, whereas the proposed topological framework captures global geometric structures of the attractor, such as the presence or disappearance of persistent cycles. Therefore, the additional com-putational cost is justified by the complementary information provided by the method. In particular, the topological approach allows for the identification of structural features of the dynamics that are not directly accessible through classical stability indicators, making it a valuable tool for the analysis of nonlinear time series.
