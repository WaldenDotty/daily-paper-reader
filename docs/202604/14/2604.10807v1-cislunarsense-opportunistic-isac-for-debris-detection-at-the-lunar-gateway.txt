# CisLunarSense: Opportunistic ISAC for Debris Detection at the Lunar Gateway 

Haofan Dong, Student Member, IEEE, and Ozgur B. Akan, Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —We propose CisLunarSense, an opportunistic in-tegrated sensing and communication (ISAC) framework that exploits the Lunar Gateway’s Ka-band relay for monostatic debris detection, addressing the absence of cislunar space situa-tional awareness infrastructure beyond the reach of ground-based radars. Using NASA/ESA-documented system parameters with author-selected sensing settings and a CR3BP-based 9:2 near-rectilinear halo orbit model, we derive the orbit-phase-dependent Cram´ er–Rao bound under OFDM inter-carrier interference, quantify a 36 dB cislunar sensing advantage over a ground-based Ka-band reference, and design a velocity-adaptive processor with mode switching at 337 m/s. Gateway operational debris (vrel < 50 m/s) is detectable within 700 km with over 30 minutes of warning; external threats ( vrel up to 500 m/s) remain detectable within 400–630 km. An orbit-phase-adaptive allocation reduces the sensing duty cycle from 60% to 19%, increasing relay throughput from 44 to 90 Mbps. A closed-form sensing outage probability for K-CPI non-coherent integration under Swerling I fluctuation shows that the 10%-outage detection range reaches 91% of the deterministic maximum at the nominal operating point K = 16 .

Index Terms —Opportunistic ISAC, cislunar space situational awareness, Lunar Gateway, OFDM radar, near-rectilinear halo orbit, space debris detection, monostatic radar. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The Lunar Gateway, scheduled for NRHO assembly around the Earth–Moon L2 point beginning in 2028 [1], faces a debris hazard with no existing surveillance capability. Objects separated during routine operations remain in the NRHO vicinity for 30–100 days, repeatedly approaching at 30–50 m/s [2], [3], beyond the reach of ground-based radars at cislunar distances [4], [5]. Integrated sensing and communication (ISAC) [6], [7], where a dual-function waveform carries data and enables radar sensing, offers a potential solution. OFDM-based ISAC is a leading 6G candidate [8], [9]. Dual-function radar-communication (DFRC) systems have been studied extensively for terrestrial applications [10]–[12]; space extensions include THz ISAC for LEO [13], bistatic LEO detection [14], and rate-splitting ISAC [15], none of which address cislunar channels or debris trajectory modeling (Table I). We consider the Lunar Gateway operating in a 9:2 synodic resonant near-rectilinear halo orbit (NRHO) about the Earth– Moon L2 point. The HALO Lunar Communication System (HLCS), built by Thales Alenia Space for ESA, serves as the primary communication relay between the lunar surface and Earth [16]. CisLunarSense exploits the HLCS Ka-band relay for opportunistic monostatic debris sensing during active relay sessions, requiring only a multicarrier-capable transceiver and allocating a configurable fraction of session time to sensing.

Remark 1 (Monostatic TDD Operation): At R = 100 km the echo is 176 dB below the transmit power, far exceed-ing Ka-band self-interference cancellation limits (65–100 dB) [20]; full-duplex is therefore infeasible. We adopt TDD consis-tent with the HLCS link structure: each relay session alternates between communication transmission bursts and dedicated sensing receive intervals of total duration ρT obs (Proposi-tion 3). During each sensing interval, the transmitter is silent and the receiver captures echoes of the immediately preceding transmission burst. The TX–RX guard interval ( ∼10 μs) sets the minimum detectable range Rmin ≈ 1.5 km; Rmax is determined by the link budget (11). 

B. Cislunar Channel Model 

The cislunar channel differs from terrestrial and LEO sce-narios in three respects: (i) pure free-space propagation with 3

R−4 path loss and no atmospheric or ionospheric attenuation; (ii) a low system noise temperature Tsys ≈ 200 K, set by the CMB (2.725 K) and a Ka-band LNA with 2.0 dB noise figure [21], versus ∼290 K for ground-based receivers; and (iii) zero ground clutter, which in LEO can raise the effective noise floor by 20–30 dB. 

Remark 2 (Cislunar Environmental Advantage): Relative to a ground-based Ka-band radar attempting the same detection task—the operational baseline for Earth-based SSA that fails at cislunar distances [4], [5]—the combined effect of these three factors provides an advantage of approximately 36 dB, decomposed as: atmospheric absorption (2 × 3.0 = 6.0 dB two-way), ionospheric scintillation (2 × 1.5 = 3 .0 dB two-way), ground clutter (25.0 dB), and thermal noise reduction ( 10 log 10 (290 /200) = 1 .6 dB). Under the R4 path loss law, this translates to a 7.8× improvement in maximum detection range over a co-located ground-based reference system. A space-to-space LEO ISAC configuration would already capture the atmospheric, ionospheric, and clutter terms; the residual cislunar advantage over such a configuration arises primarily from the absence of RF traffic congestion and is on the order of a few dB. For targets of interest ( d ≥ 0.3 m), x = πd/λ ≥ 85 places scattering in the optical regime with σ ≈ π(d/ 2) 2 [22]; the NASA SEM gives ±4 dB (1 σ) RCS uncertainty (sensitivity in Section VI). Tumbling debris is modeled as Swerling I as a conservative single-parameter baseline: the slow-fluctuation condition TCPI ≪ 1/(2 ftumble ) holds with > 10 × margin for nominal CPI durations and 0.1–10 rpm tumbling [23], [24], justifying constant RCS within a CPI; class-I (versus class-III) is adopted to avoid optimistic detection-threshold predictions for morphologies with multiple comparable scatter-ers. Three modeling simplifications warrant note: lunar/Earth-limb backscatter is geometrically excluded by NRHO altitude and > 20 ◦ off-limb antenna pointing for R > 1000 km targets, and is suppressed below the noise floor in the 400 –

1000 km headline regime by the antenna sidelobe envelope (> 30 dB below boresight at > 2 θbw off-axis); spacecraft-body multipath is reduced (but not eliminated) by HLCS two-axis gimbal pointing within allowed structural exclusion sectors; detailed clutter/multipath modeling is left to mission-stage analysis.

The CRB analysis in Section III reveals that sensing per-formance degrades near perilune due to ICI from high relative velocity. As shown in Section VI, Gateway’s own operational debris operates in the low-velocity regime ( vrel < 50 m/s) where standard processing suffices. The adaptive design devel-oped below targets the higher-velocity regime ( vrel > 100 m/s) anticipated from non-cooperative objects as cislunar traffic grows [5]. This section addresses the degradation through two mechanisms: velocity-adaptive waveform processing (Sec-tion IV-A–IV-B) and orbit-adaptive resource allocation (Sec-tion IV-C). 

A. Dual-Mode Processing Mode A (standard 2D-FFT [26]) yields GA = N M ·G (vrel );at apolune ( vrel ≈ 1–10 m/s), G ≈ ηimpl and the full gain is available. A. CR3BP-Based Recontact Trajectory Model 

The NRHO’s marginal instability ( ν ≈ 1.3 [28]) keeps separated objects ( ∆v = 1 –5 m/s) in the vicinity for 30– 100 days, with recontact every ∼3.3 days near perilune [2]. Both trajectories are propagated in the Earth–Moon CR3BP (μ = 0 .01215 ) [29] using a differentially corrected 9:2 NRHO [30], integrated (DOP853, 10 −12 tolerance) for 30–45 days. Solar radiation pressure is neglected: for compact debris (area-to-mass ratio < 0.01 m2/kg), SRP perturbation is below 1% of the CR3BP acceleration at lunar distance. For high-AMR fragments (e.g., multilayer insulation), SRP can significantly alter recontact timing and is deferred to future work. Davis et al. [2] validated CR3BP recontact predictions against full ephemeris for the first several revolutions. A campaign over 24 separation phases, 3 directions (VNB frame), and ∆v ∈ { 1, 5} m/s shows that perilune encounters amplify vrel by up to 50 × ∆v while apolune encounters yield 

vrel ≈ ∆v; normal-direction separation is the most persistent, and ∆v ≥ 5 m/s in the velocity direction eliminates recontact. The resulting vrel (θ) profile feeds the detection analysis in Section VI.

The debris returns to the Gateway vicinity after its first perilune passage, with amplified relative velocity. 3) Long-term revisit (tsep ≈ 20 –30 days): vrel = 1–

VII. C ONCLUSION 

This paper proposed CisLunarSense, an opportunistic ISAC framework tailored to the cislunar space environment. The orbit-phase-dependent CRB reveals that NRHO velocity struc-ture couples with ICI to create two operating regimes: op-erational debris ( vrel < 50 m/s, detectable within 700 km, 

>30 min warning) and external threats ( vrel up to 500 m/s, 400–630 km). The orbit-phase-adaptive allocation reduces the duty cycle from 60% to 19% and recovers 46 Mbps of throughput (44 →90 Mbps); ∼82% of the gain comes from risk-weighted per-phase Rtarget (θ) specification and the remaining 18% from phase-by-phase adaptation unique to eccentric orbits (Fig. 8). The K-CPI sensing outage probability (Proposition 5) shows that, at the nominal operating point 

K = 16 , RCS fluctuation costs less than 10% of the deter-ministic detection range, confirming that fluctuation is not the binding design constraint at operationally relevant integration depths. The framework scales with the evolving cislunar threat environment without dedicated radar hardware, requiring only multicarrier-capable relay transceivers. Future work includes 13
