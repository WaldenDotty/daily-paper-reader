# Kozai-driven mass loss of the circumbinary disk in D9 in orbit around the supermassive black hole Sgr A* 

Yannick Badoux 1, Lucas Pouw 1, Tim van der Vuurst 1, and Simon Portegies Zwart 1

Leiden Observatory, Leiden University, PO Box 9513, 2300 RA, Leiden, The Netherlands e-mail: spz@strw.leidenuniv.nl 

Received <set by editor>, 2025 / Accepted <set by editor>, 2025 

ABSTRACT 

Context. The supermassive black hole (Sgr A*) in the Galactic center is surrounded by the S-star cluster consisting of young stars on eccentric orbits. Recently, Peißker et al. (2024a) reported the detection of an S-star binary system, called D9, motivated by a periodicity in its Brackett γ (Br γ) emission. Since Br γ is considered a signature of accretion, this emission could originate from the interaction of a binary and its circumbinary disk. However, due to the gravitational interaction between Sgr A* and the binary, the disk could be short-lived. 

Aims. We investigate the evolution of the disk around a stellar binary while orbiting Sgr A*. 

Methods. We use the AMUSE framework for coupling a gravity solver (for the binary and Sgr. A*) with a hydrodynamics solver (for the disk). 

Results. We find that, irrespective of the initial disk inner and outer radii, it eventually settles between 5.2 ain and 0.28 Hill radii of the binary. Here, ain is the semi-major axis of the binary D9. The inclination of the circumbinary disk follows that of the binary, which evolves due to the von Zeipel-Lidov-Kozai (vZLK) mechanism induced by Sgr A*. The mean eccentricity of the disk is approximately in anti-phase with the eccentricity evolution of the binary. We find a vZLK timescale of TvZLK ≈ 62 .5 kyr, which is two orders of magnitude shorter than the value reported by Peisker et al. (2024). As a consequence, D9 has undergone multiple vZLK oscillations in its lifetime of 2.7 Myr. We find the disk shows periodic bursts of mass loss on the vZLK timescale, suggesting that the mass loss itself is in part driven by the vZLK mechanism. 

Conclusions. The secular evolution observed in both the binary and the disk are consistent with theoretical predictions. We find the disk loses ∼7% ± 2% of its mass every vZLK cycle. If we extrapolate this mass loss, the disk will have 1% of its current mass left after another ∼4 Myr. D9 will then be ∼6.7 Myr old, which is on the same order as the current average age of S cluster members. The vZLK-driven mass loss could, therefore, explain the absence of Br γ emission from other S cluster members. 

Key words. Accretion, accretion disks - Galaxy: center - Stars: kinematics and dynamics - Celestial mechanics 

1. Introduction 

The central parsec of the Galactic center is a dynamical environment, with at its core the supermassive black hole (SMBH) Sagittarius (Sgr) A* (Eckart & Genzel 1996; Ghez et al. 1998). Sgr A* is surrounded by the S-star cluster that contains high-velocity stars on elliptical orbits (Ghez et al. 2008; Gillessen et al. 2009). Recently, Peißker et al. (2024a) reported the detection of an S-star binary system, called D9. The primary of D9 is characterized as a Herbig Ae star and is therefore ex-pected to have a gaseous circumstellar disk (Mannings & Sargent 2000). The secondary would be a T-Tauri star. The binary nature of D9 was hypothesized because of the peri-odicity in its Brackett γ (Br γ) emission, which is associated with accretion processes of Herbig Ae/Be and T-Tauri stars (Muzerolle et al. 1998; Grant et al. 2022). Peißker et al. (2024a) offer three pathways to produce periodically varying Br γ emission in a binary system: (i) 

The secondary disturbs the circumprimary disk. (ii) There is a circumbinary disk from which gas periodically accretes onto the binary. (iii) The winds of the secondary star in-teract with a circumprimary or circumbinary disk, causing varying resonance-intercombination lines to shift (Friedjung et al. 2010). The circumprimary or circumbinary nature of the de-tected disk in D9 is uncertain, because a circumbinary disk may obscure the circumprimary disk. However, a hint may be offered by the inferred low disk mass of Mdisk ≃

1.61 ·10 −6 M⊙. Peißker et al. (2024a) speculate that the low mass may be explained by a possible formation scenario of the system, where a molecular cloud migrates towards Sgr A* during which D9 could have formed with a circumbinary disk. During the 2.7+1 .9

Myr lifetime of D9, this disk photo-evaporates due to stellar winds, until it has the present-day low mass. Peißker et al. (2024a) also discuss the future of D9. They argue that SMBH-induced von Zeipel-Lidov-Kozai (vZLK) oscillations (von Zeipel 1910; Lidov 1962; Kozai 1962; Naoz 2016) with a period comparable to the age of D9 will cause the system to merge in the near future (Stephan et al. 2016). Peißker et al. (2024a) argues that D9 is observed at a special moment in time, just before the binary merges and before the circustellar disk is fully evaporated. Furthermore, the circumbinary disk is subject to both the gravitational

We show that the disk can remain stable for a long time. We also find that the vZLK effect will not cause a merger of the binary. Overall, this makes the discovery of D9 more probable. 

2. Methods 

2.1. Computational set-up 

The dynamics of the binary system orbiting the SMBH is handled by the gravitational N-body code Hermite (Makino 1991). To simulate the dynamics of the disk, we use the Smoothed-Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) code Fi (Hern-quist & Katz 1989; Gerritsen & Icke 1997; Pelupessy et al. 2004). The Fi timestep is set to ∼ 4 days, which is 1% of the binary period. The gravito-hydrodynamic simulation is implemented with the Astronomical Multipurpose Soft-ware Environment ( AMUSE ) (Portegies Zwart et al. 2009, 2013; Pelupessy et al. 2013; Portegies Zwart et al. 2026). The gravity and hydrodynamics solvers are coupled with a Bridge (Fujii et al. 2007). We use the classic second-order bridge integration scheme in AMUSE . A higher order Bridge is not needed in our case, as Fi is a second-order integrator. The Bridge timestep is set to 10 times the hydrodynamics solver timestep ( ∼ 40 days). We set up particle sinks on both stars and Sgr A* that can catch infalling SPH particles. We find no accretion onto either the stars or Sgr A*.

Crossing the Roche limit of the stars on an eccentric orbit is a natural result of the vZLK process (Stephan et al. 2016). In some systems, eccentric mass transfer can even excite eccentricity (Rocha et al. 2025), potentially explaining the observed inner bi-nary eccentricity and the circumbinary disk. Additionally, in some cases, tides and spin can even lead to further expan-sion of the system after mass transfer (Cheng et al. 2019). In this scenario, the circumbinary disk is a consequence of the evolution (i.e., the mass transfer) rather than the ini-tial condition. Additionally, a wider initial separation for the inner orbit could have led to stronger vZLK oscilla-tions, rendering the system we’re seeing now on the track to merge (or shrink) rather than two detached point masses on a stable orbit around each other.

The eccentricity distribution of the disk particles shows the same periodicity on the vZLK timescale, though ap-proximately in anti-phase with the binary. There is also a shorter-period fluctuation visible with a periodicity of about 2 kyr when the binary has its highest eccentricity, and increasing to about 4 kyr when less eccentric. Such a quasi periodicity could be an indication of Hopf bifurcation in the disc, which would indicate a chaotic response on the disc, as its quasi periodicity is driven by the orbiting stars. But it could be a simulation artifact of the disk stabilizing due to the way it was initialized, because these minor fluc-tuations dampen over time, smoothing out after one vZLK cycle. The second panel of Fig. 3 shows the inclination over time. The peaks of the binary inclination coincide with the peaks of the binary eccentricity, which is in line with the expected behavior. We see the bulk of the disk traces the inclination variations of the binary. The third panel of Fig. 3 shows the argument of periap-sis over time. The bulk of the disk again roughly follows the evolution of the argument of periapsis of the binary. We also see shorter timescale oscillations, similar to the oscillations in the eccentricity evolution. 

3.2. vZLK-driven disk mass loss 

The bottom panel of Fig. 3 shows the number of bound disk particles over time. As seen in our preliminary runs (see sect. 2), the disk undergoes an initial period of rapid mass loss after which it settles in a quasi-stable configu-ration where it exhibits periodic bursts of mass loss on a timescale similar to TvZLK . This suggests that the mass loss is driven by the vZLK mechanism. The bursts coincide with the eccentricity distribution of the disk extending to higher values, as seen in the top panel of Fig. 3. 

4. Discussion 

4.1. The mass evolution of the circumbinary disk 

We have shown that a disk orbiting the D9 binary period-ically loses mass on a timescale corresponding to TvZLK =62 .5 kyr. Each secular cycle, the disk loses 7% ± 2% of its mass. If we assume that mass loss persisted throughout the estimated binary system lifetime of 2.7+1 .9 

> −0.3

Myr, we arrive at an initial disk mass of Mdisk = 1 .4 · 10 −4 M⊙. Since we do not account for other sources of mass loss, such as stellar winds, this is a lower bound on the initial disk mass. To verify our results, we ran two additional simulations using the same parameters as in table 1 except for the disk mass which we set to Mdisk = 1 .4 · 10 −4 M⊙ and Mdisk =1.5·10 −5 M⊙. The former is the initial mass estimate based on preliminary results (as shown in Fig. 2) and the latter being the geometric mean of this estimated initial mass and the observed disk mass. Changing the initial disk mass does not affect our results. 

4.2. Lifetime of D9 

Assuming the disk loses 7% of its mass every TvZLK =62 .5 kyr, the disk retains 1% of its current mass after ∼4Myr, at which point detection is unlikely. D9 will then be 

∼6.7 Myr old. Therefore, if D9 is born with a circumbinary disk, we estimate the time window within which the disk of D9 is detectable to be ∼6.7 Myr. This is an upper limit, because we disregard other sources of mass loss, such as photo-evaporation (Alexander 2012) or close gravitational encounters with other objects in the Galactic center. The absence of Br γ emission around other stars in the S-cluster can be explained by vZLK-driven mass loss of a circumbi-nary disk, because the average age of massive early-type S cluster members is ∼4-6 Myr (Lu et al. 2013; Habibi et al.

4.3. The potential future merger of the binary D9 

The vZLK effect can drive binaries to highly eccentric or-bits, possibly decreasing the binary separation to the regime of Roche-lobe overflow and subsequent merger. Peißker et al. (2024a) argue that, if the vZLK effect is strong enough to drive D9 to Roche-lobe overflow within a vZLK timescale, this is expected in the next vZLK timescale. They hypothesize that the population of G objects con-sists of pre-merger binaries (such as D9) and post-merger products (D9 merger products) (Peißker et al. 2024b). D9 has undergone many vZLK cycles during its esti-mated lifetime. However, since we model the stars in D9 as point masses and neglect stellar evolution, we do not allow for a merger of the binary in our simulations. The stars in our simulation never come closer to each other than 

(1 − emax )ain ≳ 4R⋆. Tidal effects may have played a role in the past evolution of D9. Such tidal evolution would nat-urally lead to the circularization of the binary. The current relatively high eccentricity suggests that tides are not im-portant for this system in the time scale of its lifetime. We test the effects of tides in D9 by simulating the evolution of the system using a secular approach including the tidal evolution of the stars (Hamers & Portegies Zwart 2016). We do this using the SecularMultiple code through the AMUSE interface (Portegies Zwart et al. 2013). For both stellar radii, we conservatively 1 adopt 2 R⊙. We find no appreciable change in the secular evolution of the orbital elements of the inner binary over several vZLK timescales, compared to our direct N-body simulations. We conclude that the vZLK effect will not drive the binary D9 to merger. 

5. Summary and conclusions 

We simulated the recently discovered S-star binary in the Galactic center (Peißker et al. 2024a), with its circumbinary disk. Our calculations are performed using AMUSE to couple a smoothed particles hydrodynamics code with a direct N-body code. We assume that the binary D9 is on a stable orbit, however, it is also possible that the observed orbital parameters and disk are the result an earlier episode of eccentric mass transfer rather than initial condition. Based on the stable orbit scenario, we conclude that: 1. The binary D9 experiences vZLK oscillations with a pe-riod of TvZLK ≈ 62.5 kyr, during which the eccentricity varies between emin ≈ 0.20 and emax ≈ 0.97. This is consistent with theory. 2. The orbital elements of the disk particles show a peri-odicity on the vZLK timescale. The eccentricity distri-bution of circumbinary disk material is widest when the binary is at its minimum eccentricity. The distributions of inclination, argument of periapsis and longitude of the ascending node trace the secular evolution of the binary.
