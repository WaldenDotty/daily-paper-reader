# Bounding Transient Instability in Sensor Data Injected Nonlinear Stochastic Flight Dynamics 

Surya Ratna Prakash D 

U R Rao Satellite Centre, ISRO, Bengaluru, India Computational and Data Sciences, Indian Institute of Science, Bengaluru, India. 

Soumyendu Raha 

Computational and Data Sciences, Indian Institute of Science, Bengaluru, India. 

Transient instability in nonlinear stochastic dynamical systems constitutes a fundamental and often overlooked limitation in safety-critical aerospace applications, particularly during powered descent and landing where failure is driven by finite-time excursions rather than asymptotic divergence. Consequently, classical notions of mean-square or asymptotic stability are insufficient for certification and design. This paper develops a logarithmic-norm-based framework for finite-time transient stability analysis of nonlinear ItГҙ stochastic differential equations. By extending matrix measures to nonlinear mappings in a Lipschitz sense, the proposed approach provides a computationally efficient characterization of instantaneous perturbation growth without requiring local lin-earization. Application of ItГҙ calculus to the logarithmic perturbation norm yields explicit bounds on the mean and variance of transient growth, establishing sufficient conditions for non-positive finite-time mean growth together with probabilistic bounds on transient instability events. The analysis reveals a fundamental distinction between mean and sample-path behavior, showing that stability in expectation does not imply pathwise finite-time safety and that almost-sure transient stability cannot, in general, be guaranteed in the presence of stochastic diffusion. The framework is further extended to data-constrained and projected stochastic dynamics arising in navigation and estimation, revealing a structural trade-off between estimation consistency and transient robustness induced by continuous data injection. The approach is demonstrated using flight-like lunar lander descent telemetry. Results show that trajectories with similar mean behavior can exhibit markedly different transient stability characteristics, and that mission failure correlates with the accumulation of transient instability over a short critical time interval. These findings establish transient instability as a structural property of stochastic, data-driven systems and motivate the use of probabilistic finite-time stability metrics for the analysis and design of safety-critical autonomous systems.

# I. Introduction 

Transient instability in nonlinear stochastic dynamical systems constitutes a fundamental limitation in safety-critical aerospace applications, particularly during powered descent and landing, where failure is driven by finite-time excursions rather than asymptotic divergence. Classical stability analysis of stochastic systems has largely focused on asymptotic or moment-based notions of stability, typically established through Lyapunov methods or second-moment bounds [ 1, 2]. Although mathematically well developed, these notions are insufficient for certification and design in safety-critical settings, as they do not capture finite-time amplification of perturbations along individual sample paths. Recent developments in control theory have begun to address finite-time behavior in stochastic systems. Finite-time stability has been investigated using Lyapunov-based approaches, particularly in discrete-time settings, where conditions for stability in probability and optimal finite-time stabilization have been established [ 3 ]. In parallel, finite-time and fixed-time stabilization have been studied in nonlinear deterministic systems [4]. However, continuous-time nonlinear stochastic systems remain significantly more challenging to analyze, and explicit characterizations of finite-time perturbation growth are still limited. A complementary perspective is provided by contraction and incremental stability analysis, which characterize stability through trajectory-wise perturbation growth rather than equilibrium-based arguments. The foundational results on incremental stability [ 5 ] and contraction analysis [ 6] have been extended to nonlinear systems using differential and Lyapunov-based frameworks [ 7, 8]. Extensions to stochastic systems have also been developed, including discrete-time contraction formulations and differentially positive systems [ 9, 10 ]. Recent work has introduced stochastic logarithmic Lipschitz constants to analyze the contractivity of stochastic differential equations [ 11 ] in a limited sense. However, these approaches primarily establish contractivity conditions and do not provide a finite-time probabilistic characterization of transient perturbation growth. Recent developments have explored contraction-based methods for nonlinear systems [ 12 ] and finite-time stability for stochastic systems ItГҙ [ 13 ]. While these approaches provide important insights into stability properties, they do not 2explicitly capture the finite-time stochastic evolution of perturbations in continuous-time nonlinear systems. Despite substantial progress in stochastic stability analysis, a critical gap remains in the development of computa-tionally tractable methods that explicitly characterize finite-time perturbation growth in continuous-time nonlinear ItГҙ systems. Existing approaches primarily provide asymptotic or moment-based guarantees [ 1, 2 ] and do not quantify the finite-time probabilistic behavior of perturbations, particularly in the presence of stochastic diffusion and data-driven corrections. In particular, contraction-based and Lyapunov-based methods ensure stability in expectation or in moments but do not capture transient amplification along individual sample paths over finite horizons, which is often the dominant mechanism of failure in safety-critical systems. From an application perspective, modern navigation and estimation architectures introduce additional complexity through continuous data injection, filtering, and constraint enforcement. In aerospace systems such as powered planetary descent, guidance strategies based on convex optimization [ 14 ] and data-driven sensing approaches [ 15 ] operate in the presence of stochastic disturbances and measurement noise. These systems are more accurately described as stochastic hybrid or data-constrained dynamical systems, for which stability properties are significantly influenced by the interaction between intrinsic dynamics and observation-driven corrections [ 16 вҖ“18 ]. In particular, projection and constraint enforcement mechanisms may introduce non-smooth corrections that alter local contraction properties and can induce transient amplification even when the underlying dynamics are locally contractive. The impact of such data injection mechanisms on finite-time transient stability remains insufficiently understood, especially in continuous-time nonlinear settings. In strongly transient mission phases such as spacecraft entry, descent, and landing, the primary safety requirement is the avoidance of unsafe excursions during a finite-duration maneuver rather than asymptotic convergence. In this regime, systems that are stable in expectation or in moments may nevertheless exhibit significant transient amplification along individual sample paths [ 2 ]. Such events, although probabilistically rare, can have catastrophic consequences and must therefore be explicitly characterized in analysis and certification. Transient stochastic stability has also been investigated for linear systems using stochastic logarithmic norms [ 19 ], highlighting the importance of finite-time behavior. Extension of [ 19 ] to Lipschitz-type coefficients and Stratonovich formulations is found in [ 11 ]. However, these early versions of stochastic logarithmic norm require the weak derivative of the Wiener process, a restriction on the growth bound of the perturbation. To address this gap, this paper develops a logarithmic-norm-based framework for finite-time transient stability analysis of nonlinear ItГҙ stochastic differential equations without requiring weak derivative assumptions on the Wiener process. Logarithmic norms, also known as matrix measures in the linear setting, characterize infinitesimal perturbation growth [ 20 , 21 ]. By extending this concept to nonlinear mappings in a Lipschitz sense, the proposed formulation characterizes perturbation dynamics directly at the level of nonlinear stochastic flows without relying on variational linearization. Unlike classical approaches, the method avoids both local linearization and Lyapunov function construction, 3operating directly on finite perturbations in a computationally tractable manner. A key outcome of the analysis is a decomposition of transient stability behavior into a mean growth component and a diffusion-induced variability component, characterized by the quantities рқӣј and рқӣҪ , respectively. This decomposition provides a direct link between deterministic contraction properties and stochastic variability in finite-time stability. Unlike contraction-based approaches, which provide sufficient conditions for incremental stability, the present work establishes sufficient conditions for non-positive mean transient growth together with probabilistic bounds on transient instability events. In particular, the analysis shows that stability in expectation does not guarantee pathwise finite-time safety and that stochastic diffusion fundamentally induces a nonzero probability of transient growth over arbitrarily small time intervals. This establishes a structural limitation of stochastic stability that is particularly relevant for safety-critical systems. The main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

вҖў A logarithmic-norm-based framework for nonlinear ItГҙ stochastic differential equations that characterizes instantaneous perturbation growth in a Lipschitz sense without requiring local linearization. 

вҖў Explicit finite-time bounds on the mean and variance of logarithmic perturbation growth, yielding sufficient conditions for non-positive mean transient growth together with probabilistic bounds on transient instability events. 

вҖў A fundamental limitation result showing that stability in expectation does not guarantee pathwise finite-time safety, and that stochastic diffusion induces a strictly nonzero probability of transient growth over arbitrarily small time intervals. 

вҖў Extension to data-constrained and projected stochastic dynamics, revealing a structural trade-off between estimation consistency and transient robustness induced by continuous data injection. 

вҖў Demonstration on flight-like lunar lander descent telemetry, showing that systems with similar mean behavior can exhibit markedly different transient stability characteristics, with failure driven by accumulated transient instability. These results indicate that transient instability in nonlinear stochastic systems is not merely a design artifact but a structural consequence of stochastic diffusion and continuous data injection. This motivates a shift from asymptotic stability analysis toward probabilistic finite-time stability metrics for the analysis and certification of safety-critical autonomous systems.

D. Transient Stability Objective 

In contrast to classical asymptotic or mean-square stability notions, the focus of this paper is on finite-time transient behavior. Specifically, we study the stochastic evolution of the logarithmic perturbation norm ln вҲҘрқӣҝрқ‘Ӣ рқ‘Ў вҲҘ рқ‘қ , and seek conditions under which its expected growth and probabilistic excursions can be bounded over finite-time intervals. This formulation is motivated by its connection to multiplicative growth rates and finite-time Lyapunov exponents, 6providing a natural measure of transient amplification. It captures transient instability phenomena that may arise even when asymptotic or moment stability conditions are satisfied, consistent with sample-path perspectives in stochastic dynamical systems [ 2 ]. Moreover, it enables a coordinate-invariant characterization of finite-time sensitivity without requiring Lyapunov function constructions or moment-based analysis. We restrict attention to perturbations satisfying вҲҘрқӣҝрқ‘Ӣ рқ‘Ў вҲҘ рқ‘қ > 0 almost surely over the interval of interest, ensuring that the logarithmic perturbation measure is well defined almost surely. This formulation naturally leads to a decomposition of transient behavior into mean growth and diffusion-induced variability components, which will be quantified through the parameters рқӣј and рқӣҪ in the subsequent analysis. 

# III. Logarithmic Norm in the Lipschitz Sense 

This section introduces a logarithmic norm for nonlinear mappings in a Lipschitz sense, extending classical matrix measures to nonlinear and stochastic settings. The proposed construction provides an efficient characterization of instantaneous perturbation growth and serves as the central analytical tool for the transient stability analysis developed in subsequent sections. Logarithmic norms, also known as matrix measures in the linear setting, were originally introduced in numerical stability analysis [ 20 ] and later adopted in nonlinear systems theory [ 21 ]. Throughout this paper, stochastic differential equations are interpreted in the ItГҙ sense following standard conventions [1]. The constructions in this section are formulated in a finite-difference and upper Dini derivative sense, rather than requiring classical FrГ©chet differentiability. This allows the framework to remain valid for locally Lipschitz nonlinear mappings without invoking variational linearization. In this setting, the logarithmic norm quantifies the instantaneous worst-case rate of perturbation growth along admissible perturbation directions, with positive values indicating local expansion and negative values indicating contraction.

# VII. Discussion and Design Implications 

This section distills the practical implications of the transient stability framework developed in Sections IIIвҖ“VI for the analysis, certification, and design of safety-critical autonomous systems operating under stochastic uncertainty and continuous data injection. 

A. Finite-Time Safety versus Mean Stability 

The analysis indicates that finite-time safety cannot, in general, be reliably inferred from asymptotic or mean stability criteria alone. For safety-critical missions operating in strongly transient regimes, certification objectives may therefore be formulated in terms of bounded finite-time excursion probabilities rather than asymptotic convergence or moment stability. This perspective reframes transient instability as a risk management problem rather than a purely stabilization problem, aligning stability analysis with operational safety requirements in mission-critical phases such as entry, descent, and landing. 

B. Structural Impact of Data Injection and Projection 

Continuous data injection and projection-based correction mechanisms, while essential for estimation consistency and constraint enforcement, structurally modify the intrinsic contraction properties of the system dynamics and introduce additional sources of transient amplification. This effect arises from the geometry of the constrained dynamics and is largely independent of specific algorithmic implementations under the considered modeling assumptions. Consequently, navigation and estimation architectures should be analyzed at the level of their induced projected dynamics rather than solely through nominal system models. This observation suggests a trade-off between estimation consistency and transient robustness that must be explicitly managed in safety-critical systems. 

C. Implications for Navigation System Certification 

The results motivate a shift in certification philosophy for autonomous navigation systems. In particular: 

вҖў certification criteria should incorporate probabilistic finite-time excursion bounds rather than relying solely on deterministic asymptotic stability guarantees, 

вҖў safety margins should be formulated in terms of allowable transient amplification probabilities, 

вҖў navigation and estimation architectures should be evaluated under projection-modified dynamics rather than nominal models, 

вҖў online monitoring of transient stability indicators should be integrated into safety supervision layers. 27 Such criteria more accurately reflect the stochastic and data-driven nature of modern navigation systems operating under continuous data injection. 

D. Relation to Contraction-Based Design 

Classical contraction theory provides powerful sufficient conditions for incremental stability under deterministic dynamics [ 6, 12 ], and stochastic extensions characterize mean contraction properties [ 22 ]. The present framework complements these approaches by explicitly characterizing finite-time transient amplification mechanisms induced by stochastic diffusion and observation noise. This suggests that contraction properties in stochastic systems can be interpreted as average or probabilistic stability notions rather than guarantees of pathwise behavior in safety-critical regimes. 

E. Design Guidelines 

From a design standpoint, the analysis motivates the following structural guidelines: 

вҖў it is beneficial to prefer bounded and smooth projection operators to mitigate curvature-induced transient amplification, 

вҖў tune data injection gains with explicit consideration of transient risk rather than steady-state estimation accuracy alone, 

вҖў integrate transient stability indicators as real-time risk monitors during critical mission phases, 

вҖў couple projection-based estimation with risk-aware control or supervisory logic to attenuate finite-time excursions. These guidelines identify structural design considerations rather than prescribing specific algorithms and are applicable across navigation, estimation, and control architectures subject to continuous data injection. 

Fundamental Implication Transient instability in nonlinear stochastic systems can arise as a structural consequence of stochastic diffusion and continuous data injection over finite-time horizons. Consequently, the objective of analysis and design shifts from elimination of transient instability to the principled quantification, monitoring, and management of transient risk in safety-critical autonomous systems. 

F. Practical Use of Logarithmic Norm Metrics in GN&C Design 

The quantities рқӣј (рқ‘Ў ) and рқӣҪ (рқ‘Ў ) derived in Section V are not intended to provide tight numerical estimates of failure probability. Instead, they serve as computationally efficient indicators of transient instability risk over finite-time horizons. Here, рқӣј (рқ‘Ў ) represents the mean logarithmic growth rate, while рқӣҪ (рқ‘Ў ) characterizes diffusion-induced variability. These quantities correspond directly to the mean and variance bounds on logarithmic perturbation growth developed in Section V, where рқӣј (рқ‘Ў ) governs expected contraction and рқӣҪ (рқ‘Ў ) quantifies diffusion-induced dispersion. Conclusion 

This paper develops a logarithmic-norm-based framework for finite-time transient stability analysis of nonlinear ItГҙ stochastic differential equations, with direct relevance to safety-critical autonomous navigation and estimation. By extending classical matrix measures to nonlinear mappings in a Lipschitz sense, the framework provides a computationally efficient characterization of instantaneous perturbation growth and enables finite-time analysis without reliance on Lyapunov constructions or local linearization. Explicit bounds on the mean and variance of logarithmic perturbation growth are derived, yielding sufficient conditions for non-positive mean transient growth together with probabilistic bounds on transient instability events. The analysis establishes a fundamental distinction between mean and pathwise behavior: stability in expectation does not guarantee pathwise finite-time safety, and almost-sure transient stability cannot, in general, be ensured in the presence of stochastic diffusion. The quantities рқӣј and рқӣҪ provide a natural decomposition of these effects, characterizing mean contraction and diffusion-induced variability, respectively. The framework is further extended to data-constrained and projected stochastic dynamics arising in navigation and estimation. The results show that continuous data injection structurally modifies contraction properties and introduces additional diffusion, thereby increasing the likelihood of transient amplification. This reveals an inherent, algorithm-independent trade-off between estimation consistency and transient robustness. Numerical results using flight-like lunar lander descent telemetry demonstrate that finite-time perturbation growth provides early indicators of instability mechanisms not captured by conventional mean stability metrics, and that failure correlates with the accumulation of transient instability over a short critical time window. 

Practical Implications for GN&C Design The results suggest several actionable considerations for guidance, navigation, and control system design: 

вҖў monitor the transient stability indicator рқӣј (рқ‘Ў ) as a real-time measure of finite-time instability risk, 

вҖў limit aggressive data injection or high-gain corrections during critical mission phases to reduce projection-induced diffusion and transient amplification, 

вҖў use cumulative transient instability as a diagnostic metric for identifying high-risk trajectories, 

вҖў incorporate transient stability screening as a pre-processing step to reduce Monte Carlo simulation burden. 

Final Implication Transient instability in nonlinear stochastic systems is not merely a modeling artifact or controller deficiency, but a structural consequence of stochastic diffusion and continuous data injection over finite-time horizons. Consequently, the objective of analysis and design for safety-critical autonomous systems must shift from eliminating transient instability to quantifying, monitoring, and managing transient risk. Future work will focus on embedding the proposed transient stability metrics into real-time risk-aware navigation, 36 estimation, and control architectures, and extending the framework to hybrid dynamics, delays, and partial observability.
