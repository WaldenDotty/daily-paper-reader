# UNRIO: Uncertainty-Aware Velocity Learning for Radar-Inertial Odometry 

Jui-Te Huang, Tinashu Huang, Anthony Rowe and Michael Kaess 

Abstract — We present UNRIO, an uncertainty-aware radar-inertial odometry system that estimates ego-velocity directly from raw mmWave radar IQ signals rather than processed point clouds. Existing radar-inertial odometry methods rely on handcrafted signal processing pipelines that discard latent information in the raw spectrum and require careful parameter tuning. To address this, we propose a transformer-based neural network built on the GRT architecture that processes the full 4-D spectral cube to predict body-frame velocity in two modes: a direct linear velocity estimate and a per-angle-bin Doppler velocity map. The network is trained in three stages — geometric pretraining on LiDAR-projected depth, velocity or Doppler fine-tuning, and uncertainty calibration via negative log-likelihood loss — enabling it to produce uncertainty estimates alongside its predictions. These uncertainty estimates are propagated into a sliding-window pose graph that fuses radar velocity factors with IMU preintegration measurements. We train and evaluate UNRIO on the IQ1M dataset across diverse indoor environments with both forward and lateral motion patterns unseen during training. Our method achieves the lowest relative pose error on the majority of sequences, with particularly strong gains over classical DSP baselines on lateral-motion trajectories where sparse point clouds degrade conventional velocity estimators. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

mmWave radar sensing technology has enabled simultane-ous localization and mapping (SLAM) in low-visibility, low-lighting, and adverse environmental conditions where LiDAR and RGB cameras fail to provide reliable sensing. Recent advances in radar state estimation [1]–[6] have demonstrated the potential of radar sensors for localization. However, all of the above works rely on signal-processed radar point clouds, while the use of raw complex radar signals for state estimation remains largely unexplored. Modern commercially available mmWave radars offer many design choices [7]. Manufacturers support a wide range of signal configurations, antenna designs, and layouts, paired with proprietary signal processing pipelines that typically produce point clouds with per-point Doppler velocity for downstream tasks. The most common approach employs frequency-modulated continuous wave (FMCW) waveforms with a multi-input multi-output (MIMO) antenna array. The signal processing algorithm usually starts with multi-dimensional Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), Constant False Alarm Rate (CFAR) detection, and angle-of-arrival estima-tion. However, this pipeline discards a significant amount of information latent in the raw signal, while also introducing numerous heuristic parameters that require careful tuning and

A. Radar-Inertial Odometry 

Since the early developments of the field, research on mmWave radar for state estimation has been using radar-generated point clouds with Doppler velocity. Works from Doer et al. [1], [13] build a baseline radar-inertial odometry system estimating body-frame velocity using RANSAC and fusing it with IMU via EKF filtering. Building on their work, Kim et al. [5] further extend the EKF with online temporal calibration. Huang et al. [4] use a fixed-lag window opti-mization strategy to fuse multiple radar-estimated velocities with IMU in an optimization window. With more antennas and higher angular resolution on recent commercial mmWave radars, other works incorporate scan-matching strategies from LiDAR state estimation sys-tems. Zhuang et al. integrated body velocity, IMU, and scan-to-map matching in their radar SLAM system: 4D iRIOM [3]. Herraez et al. [14] build a full SLAM system with radar and IMU where the main odometry sources come from radar point scan-to-map matching. Despite all these developments, no works have attempted to leverage full radar spectrum information for state estima-tion, with the closest being [15] which discusses the effect of signal processing on a radar odometry system while still relying on generated point clouds. 

B. Machine Learning for Radar Spectrum 

While most radar odometry systems — and radar percep-tion systems more broadly — rely on point clouds, point cloud extraction discards much of the information contained in a raw 4D radar cube. Competing approaches instead propose to directly interpret the cube by training neural networks with various architectures on large volumes of data, and are able to achieve superior resolution and accuracy on tasks ranging from object detection [16]–[18] and super-resolution [8], [19] to camera-like semantic segmentation [8], [20] and multi-view 3D reconstruction [21]–[23]. In particular, models trained using foundation model-style pretraining have been shown to be highly scalable with increased training data [8]. Increasingly capable edge compute systems have also made deploying ML-based radar perception to real time systems [24] much more practical. Together, this raises the possibility of using large-scale datasets to pretrain powerful, general-purpose models for radar perception, which are then distilled and deployed to autonomous robots and vehicles. 

C. Uncertainty Prediction for State Estimation 

While learning-based methods are being adopted by the SLAM research community, several works have explored learned uncertainty estimation. Teed et al. [11] propose DROID-SLAM, which utilizes a differentiable bundle ad-justment layer to implicitly tune the uncertainty model. Dexheimer et al. [25] proposed a learned depth covariance function applied in downstream tasks such as 3D recon-struction. To improve the reliability of the feature tracking process, some methods introduce learning-based uncertainty measurements or confidence scores, such as Wang et al. [10] and Murai et al. [26], which leverage learned 3D reconstruc-tion priors for dense SLAM. Similarly, Qiu et al. [9] propose MAC-VO, a learning-based stereo visual odometry frame-work that trains a metrics-aware covariance model to select keypoints and weight residuals in pose graph optimization. Similar ideas have been explored in LiDAR-based state es-timation, where Zhao et al. [27] propose SuperLoc, a robust LiDAR-inertial localization system that predicts alignment risks from raw sensor measurements before optimization. Inspired by these works, we apply the same idea of learned velocity and uncertainty estimation to radar spectrum data for state estimation. III. M ETHODOLOGY 

In this section, we present our learning-based method for processing raw IQ mmWave radar signals to estimate body-frame velocity and its uncertainty. The predicted velocity and uncertainty are then fused with IMU measurements in a sliding-window pose graph optimization to recover the odometry trajectory. 

A. Signal Processing 

In this work, we use a typical commercially avail-able mmWave radar that transmit frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) which emits a sequence of chirps across an antenna array in an multi-input multi-output (MIMO) mode. The raw output of a single frame is a complex IQ tensor of shape (Nc, N tx , N rx , N r ), where Nc

is the number of chirps (slow-time), Ntx and Nrx are the transmit and receive antenna counts, and Nr is the number of fast-time samples per chirp. To process this data, we apply a four-dimensional Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). A range FFT along the fast-time axis and a Doppler FFT along the slow-time axis produce a range-Doppler map. Subsequently, virtual aperture synthesis via elevation and azimuth FFTs yields a 4-D spectral cube 

S ∈ CND ×NE ×NA×NR , where ND , NE , NA, and NR

denote the Doppler, elevation, azimuth, and range bin counts, respectively. We pad the FFT size to a bigger number for both angular FFTs to contain more detailed angular information. After the FFTs, each complex bin re jθ is encoded as a three-channel feature (log 10 r, sinθ, cos θ), separating log-magnitude from the phase components, giving the network input X ∈ RND ×NE ×NA×NR×3. Instead of encoding the phase component as an angle in GRT [8] where values are discontinuous around −π and π. We use sin and cos of the phase angle to preserve continuity. B. Neural Network Architecture 

We build our velocity and uncertainty prediction network based on the GRT [8] transformer architecture. We experi-ment with two types of velocity prediction: one that directly outputs the linear body-frame velocity, and another that outputs per-angle-bin Doppler values. To enable uncertainty estimation, we design the network to have separate decoder heads for prediction and uncertainty outputs. All network components are listed as follows: 

Tokenizer. The spectral cube X is processed by a GRT-style spectrum tokenizer to patchify inputs into tokens. The elevation and azimuth axes are first collapsed into the channel dimension via a learned linear layer, after which spatial patches are applied over the Doppler and range axes, respectively. Each patch is projected to a d-dimensional embedding. Sinusoidal positional encodings are added, and a learned readout token is appended. The spectrum cube X

are now transformed into tokens of T ∈ R(N +1) ×d.

Encoder. A 4-layer multi-head self-attention transformer encoder processes tokens T into contextual features E.

Depth and Doppler decoder. A 4-layer Transformer decoder with a learned query grid of the desired output image of size (OE , O A), which presents required output angle bins of elevation and azimuth. The queries are then cross-attended to encoded tokens E and produce an output image with 2 channels: a normalized depth map ˆρ ∈ ROE ×OA and a normalized Doppler image ˆd ∈ ROE ×OA . Which can later be transformed to metric scale Doppler image ˆdm using maximum Doppler velocity derived from chirp configuration. 

uncertainty decoder. A separate lightweight 2-layer Transformer decoder with identical architecture produces per-pixel log-variance maps ˆℓd ∈ ROe×Oa for the Doppler. Outputs are clamped for numerical stability. 

Velocity and uncertainty heads. Two 3-layer MLPs operate on the encoder output readout token. The velocity head outputs a positive velocity scale and a unit direction. 

[s, n] ∈ R4, which then converted to body-frame velocity as ˆv = sn ∈ R3. The log-variance head outputs per-axis log-variance ˆℓv ∈ R3. log-variances are also clamped for numerical stability. 

C. Training Strategy 

In order to make the model generalize, our model training proceeds in three stages, each building on the trained model weights from the previous stage. 

Stage 1 — Depth pretraining. The tokenizer, encoder, and prediction decoder are trained to regress LiDAR-projected depth using a masked L1 loss and a spatial gradient regularizer: 

Ldepth = LL1 (ˆ ρ, ρ ⋆, V) + λg Lgrad (ˆ ρ, ρ ⋆, V), (1) where ρ⋆ is the normalized LiDAR depth by maximum radar distance, V is the valid-pixel mask within maximum radar range, and λg =0 .5 weights the gradient term. The covariance decoder and all velocity heads are frozen. In this stage, we train the network with all available data from the GRT dataset. Including three different chirp configurations, depth, and velocity patterns. This stage provides a strong geometric representation before Doppler or velocity supervision is applied. 

Stage 2 — Velocity or Doppler prediction. In this stage, we only fine-tune on our target indoor environment subset of the GRT dataset. Depends on the experiment, we either trained on velocity or Doppler output heads. For training velocity ˆv, we simply use an L1 loss with ground truth body velocity v⋆ calculated from LiDAR odometry. For training the Doppler image ˆd, we project the ground truth body velocity to each angle bin and get the ground truth Doppler image d⋆.

C. Quantitative Evaluation 

We evaluate our radar-inertial odometry using the EVO [29] library. Tables I and II report the Absolute Pose Error (APE) and Relative Pose Error (RPE) of the trans-lation component across all testing sequences, separated by dominant motion type: forward and lateral. APE is computed with Umeyama alignment, and RPE is evaluated at 10-meter intervals. Our method achieves the lowest RPE in the majority of sequences, demonstrating superior local accuracy compared to all baselines. The advantage is particularly evident in the lateral-motion sequences (Table II), where learning-based estimators consistently outperform the classical DSP baseline by a margin; with lateral motion, DSP produces fewer points along the direction of motion, whereas our approach leverages the full Doppler spectrum to maintain accuracy even when the point cloud is sparse or poorly conditioned. In forward-motion sequences (Table I), the gap narrows since radial velocity is better observed, yet our method still achieves competitive APE and the best RPE in most traces. 

D. Qualitative Evaluation 

Figure 2 shows representative trajectory comparisons across lateral and forward-motion sequences. In the lateral sequences, the DSP point-cloud baseline (PC) exhibits large drift, often deviating significantly from the ground truth. The GRT baseline shows inconsistent behavior, competitive on some traces yet failing on others. In contrast, the Doppler and Velocity estimators track the ground truth closely through-out, with the zoomed insets revealing that the Velocity method maintains tighter agreement in regions where the other methods diverge. For the forward-motion sequence (posner.2.fwd ), all methods improve, consistent with the quantitative results in Table I, as radial Doppler geometry is better conditioned in the direction of travel. Nevertheless, the most notable advantage of our learning-based estimators is in lateral-motion sequences, where they consistently maintain trajectory smoothness and accuracy while classical DSP-based methods exhibit significant drift. V. C ONCLUSION 

We presented UNRIO, a radar-inertial odometry system that estimates ego-velocity and its uncertainty directly from raw mmWave radar IQ signals, bypassing the information loss inherent in classical signal processing pipelines. Our transformer-based network supports both direct velocity and per-angle-bin Doppler output modes, each with calibrated un-certainty estimates that are propagated into a sliding-window pose graph fusing radar and IMU measurements. Evaluations on held-out indoor environments from the IQ1M dataset show that UNRIO achieves state-of-the-art relative pose error across most sequences, with the largest gains on lateral-motion trajectories where classical DSP-based point cloud methods struggle due to sparse radial observations. These results demonstrate the value of operating on the full radar spectrum for robust, generalizable state estimation.
