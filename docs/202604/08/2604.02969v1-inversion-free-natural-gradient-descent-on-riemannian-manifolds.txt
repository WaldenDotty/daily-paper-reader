# Inversion-Free Natural Gradient Descent on Riemannian Manifolds 

## Dario Draca 1, Takuo Matsubara 2 and Minh-Ngoc Tran 3

1School of Mathematics and Statistics, University of Sydney, Australia 

2School of Mathematics, University of Edinburgh, UK 

3University of Sydney Business School, Australia 

Abstract 

The natural gradient method is widely used in statistical optimization, but its standard for-mulation assumes a Euclidean parameter space. This paper proposes an inversion-free stochas-tic natural gradient method for probability distributions whose parameters lie on a Riemannian manifold. The manifold setting offers several advantages: one can implicitly enforce parameter constraints such as positive definiteness and orthogonality, ensure parameters are identifiable, or guarantee regularity properties of the objective like geodesic convexity. Building on an intrinsic formulation of the Fisher information matrix (FIM) on a manifold, our method main-tains an online approximation of the inverse FIM, which is efficiently updated at quadratic cost using score vectors sampled at successive iterates. In the Riemannian setting, these score vec-tors belong to different tangent spaces and must be combined using transport operations. We prove almost-sure convergence rates of O(log s/s α) for the squared distance to the minimizer when the step size exponent α > 2/3. We also establish almost-sure rates for the approxi-mate FIM, which now accumulates transport-based errors. A limited-memory variant of the algorithm with sub-quadratic storage complexity is proposed. Finally, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our method relative to its Euclidean counterparts on variational Bayes with Gaussian approximations and normalizing flows. 

Keywords: Variational Bayes; Fisher information matrix; Stochastic optimization; Rie-mannian optimization; Bures-Wasserstein manifold 1

# 1 Introduction 

This paper studies the optimization of an objective function L(θ) ≡ L (qθ) defined over a family of parametric distributions Q = {qθ : θ ∈ M} . Problems of this form are central to statistics and machine learning. For instance, maximum likelihood estimation minimizes the negative log-likelihood over Q, while variational Bayes approximates an intractable posterior distribution by minimizing the negative evidence lower bound (Hoffman et al. 2013). Two broad approaches exist for this class of problems. The first identifies Q with its parameter space M, reducing the optimization to a standard problem in M, but discarding the geometric structure of Q. This paper adopts the second approach, which views Q as a statistical manifold in the sense of information geometry (Amari 2016), and leverages tools from differential geometry to facilitate optimization. When Q is equipped with the Fisher-Rao metric, the Riemannian gradient of the objective is known as the natural gradient (Amari 1998). The natural gradient defines a parameterization-invariant steepest descent direction on Q, and has been shown to yield significant improvements on a broad range of optimization tasks (Hoffman et al. 2013, Tran et al. 2017, Lin et al. 2019, Martens 2020). However, most existing work is limited to problems where M is a Euclidean space. There are several reasons to consider parameter spaces with a manifold structure. First, numerous applications involve constrained domains; for example, consider the space of sym-metric and positive definite (SPD) matrices Sd

The Stiefel manifold St( p, n ) for p ≤ n encodes matrices in Rn×p with orthonormal columns (Edelman et al. 1998). Further examples include the probability simplex, fixed rank matrices (Absil & Malick 2012), correlation matrices (David & Gu 2019), and more. The second motivation is the elimination of non-identifiable parameter directions; hence, ensuring invertibility of the Fisher information matrix (FIM). In certain dimension reduction 2methods, one optimizes a likelihood L(S) where S ∈ St( p, n ) represents a linear subspace, so that L(S) = L(SO ) for any O ∈ St( p, p ). The natural setting for optimizing over linear subspaces is the Grassmann manifold (Edelman et al. 1998); examples of likelihood-based optimization on this space include sufficient dimension reduction (Cook & Forzani 2009, Nghiem et al. 2024) and envelope methods (Cook & Zhang 2015). This identifiability problem also occurs in models which incorporate low-rank matrices in the factored form α⊤β where 

α, β ∈ Rp×n for p < n ; e.g. reduced-rank vector GLMs (Yee & Hastie 2003), and models for co-integrated time series (Koop et al. 2009). The non-identifiability can be circumvented by working on the manifold of fixed rank matrices (Meyer et al. 2011, Mishra et al. 2014). The third reason concerns the regularity of the objective function. Some objective func-tions, which appear non-convex in a Euclidean setting, exhibit convexity along the geodesics of a suitably chosen manifold. The Bures-Wasserstein space (Bures 1969, Malagò et al. 2018), denoted BW( Rd), corresponds to the set of d-dimensional Gaussian distributions endowed with the 2-Wasserstein metric. The KL divergence on BW( Rd) is known to be geodesically convex when the target distribution is log-concave (Lambert et al. 2022). However, convexity does not hold under the standard Euclidean parameterization of the covariance or precision matrices (Challis & Barber 2013). Such regularity properties are useful for constructing natural gradient algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees (Kumar et al. 2025). The formulation of the Fisher information on a smooth manifold is well-established in the statistical literature, where it is viewed as a bilinear form in each tangent space of M;see e.g. Smith (2005), Xavier & Barroso (2005). Despite the growing interest in Riemannian optimization, comparatively little has been done on extending the natural gradient method to general manifolds. Prominent work includes Hu et al. (2024), whose approach applies to embedded submanifolds and quotient manifolds (Absil et al. 2009) of Rm×n, where tangent spaces can be identified with subspaces of an ambient vector space. They represent the intrinsic FIM as an r × r matrix (r = mn ) in the ambient space, and employ Kronecker-factor approximations (Martens & Grosse 2015) to reduce computational requirements for 3models with a compatible gradient structure. They establish almost-sure consistency for the iterates, and also fast local convergence rates in the deterministic setting. The main contribution of this paper is a stochastic natural gradient method for optimiza-tion over Riemannian parameter spaces. In contrast to Hu et al. (2024) 

• Our approach is purely intrinsic, does not require an embedding space, and only makes standard regularity assumptions on the manifold and objective function. 

• We explicitly treat the FIM as a stochastic object, and employ an online estimate which is asymptotically consistent under mild regularity conditions. The fundamental idea is to maintain a moving approximation of the inverse FIM, which is updated with a small number of new score vectors at each iterate. By exploiting the outer product structure of the FIM, this low-rank update can be performed in quadratic time (Amari et al. 2000, Park et al. 2000, Godichon-Baggioni et al. 2024). In the Riemannian setting, the approximate FIM is localized to the tangent space of the current iterate, and must be updated using vector transport operations (Absil et al. 2009). 

• We prove almost-sure convergence rates of O(log s/s α) for the squared distance to a local minimizer, and also provide almost-sure rates for the approximate FIM. 

• We propose a limited-memory variant of the algorithm which employs a low-rank esti-mate of the FIM, yielding sub-quadratic storage complexity. The above are inspired by Godichon-Baggioni et al. (2024), who applied this idea to variational Bayes in the Euclidean setting. The major new technical difficulty concerns the convergence analysis of the approximate FIM, which now accumulates curvature-based distortions, and also errors due to the approximation of the parallel transport operation. 

• We demonstrate the efficacy of our method on variational Bayes using Gaussian dis-tributions on BW( Rd), and normalizing flows (Berg et al. 2018) on St( p, n ).4The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the necessary preliminaries and background. In Section 3, we review the intrinsic formulation of the Fisher information on a smooth manifold, and the corresponding representation of the natural gradient. Section 4 presents our inversion-free natural gradient method, followed by a rigorous convergence anal-ysis in Section 5. We demonstrate the application of our method to the Bures-Wasserstein and Stiefel manifolds in Section 6 and Section 7, respectively. Finally, Section 8 concludes the paper. # 8 Conclusion 

This paper developed a stochastic natural gradient method for optimization over probability distributions whose parameters lie on a Riemannian manifold. This formulation accommo-dates several constrained parameter spaces which commonly arise in statistics, including SPD matrices, the Stiefel manifold, and the Grassmann manifold. We proposed a Rieman-nian extension of an inversion-free approximate natural gradient method (Amari et al. 2000, Godichon-Baggioni et al. 2024), which streamlines the adaptive estimation of the inverse Fisher information matrix. We also proposed a limited-memory variant which reduces stor-age complexity from quadratic to sub-quadratic in the manifold dimension. In contrast to previous work (Tran et al. 2021, Hu et al. 2024), our approach is purely intrinsic, and yields almost-sure convergence rates in the stochastic setting for both the parameter iterates and approximate Fisher information. Finally, we presented two extended examples which demon-strated the efficacy of this geometric approach relative to its Euclidean counterparts. An interesting continuation of our research concerns its application beyond finite-dimensional models. In particular, the Wasserstein space of probability measures possesses a formal Rie-mannian structure (Villani et al. 2008), which provides notions of gradient, geodesics, and parallel transport. This suggests a possible route toward adapting our methodology to the non-parametric setting. Applications could involve particle-based algorithms like SVGD (Liu & Wang 2016), which approximate the gradient flow of the KL divergence in the Wasserstein space (Liu 2017). 25 Competing interests
