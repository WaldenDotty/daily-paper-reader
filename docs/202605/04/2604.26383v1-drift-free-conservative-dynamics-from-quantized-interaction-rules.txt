Conservation laws are conventionally discretized through floating-point flux evaluation, with in-variants obtained by cancellation of approximate interface contributions and admissible weak solu-tions selected by reconstruction and Riemann solvers. Here we introduce an operator-level formula-tion in which conservative dynamics is realized as an exact discrete interaction rule on a quantized state space. The update is defined by an antisymmetric integer-transfer operator, which enforces conservation exactly at the arithmetic level and eliminates round-off drift from the primitive evo-lution [1]. For scalar laws, monotone order-preserving transfers select admissible shock structures within the primitive update, rather than through flux reconstruction. Numerical experiments show that the interaction rule preserves high-frequency transport near the Nyquist limit and maintains sharply localized discontinuities in Burgers dynamics. The same construction extends to multi-dimensional problems and systems of conservation laws through oriented, vector-valued integer transfers. These results indicate that conservative dynamics admits an exact discrete realization in which both invariance and entropy selection are encoded at the operator level, rather than arising from approximate flux cancellation. 

INTRODUCTION 

Conservation laws play a central role in physical mod-eling, expressing invariant quantities such as mass, mo-mentum, and energy through local balance relations. In standard numerical formulations, these laws are realized by floating-point evaluation of interface fluxes in finite-volume or finite-difference frameworks [2–7]. Conserva-tion follows from pairwise cancellation of flux contribu-tions, while admissible weak solutions are selected by re-construction procedures and Riemann solvers. Structurally, this approach represents conservative dy-namics through approximate flux evaluation in finite-precision arithmetic. Consequently, invariants are pre-served only up to machine precision, and the primitive update depends on rounding behavior, summation or-der, and reconstruction choices. Although these effects are usually small, they create a separation between the formal conservation law and its discrete realization. In this work, we take a different viewpoint: conserva-tive dynamics is realized directly as a discrete interaction process. Instead of computing approximate fluxes, we define the primitive evolution by a local antisymmetric transfer operator acting on a quantized state space [8, 9]. Conservation is then not imposed by numerical cancella-tion, but is an exact algebraic invariant of the interaction rule. The resulting method does not approximate a flux-based scheme at the primitive level; it replaces the flux representation itself by an exact antisymmetric transfer operator on a countable state space. This work does not introduce a numerical approxima-tion of a continuum equation, but formulates a primitive discrete dynamics from which the continuum description emerges. This representation has two immediate consequences. First, conservation is exact at every step and is indepen-dent of floating-point arithmetic. Second, weak-solution selection is relocated to the primitive update: entropy-admissible solutions are determined by the monotone, order-preserving structure of the transfer operator itself [10]. In this sense, the role usually attributed to Riemann solvers is not removed, but absorbed into the operator-level interaction rule. We therefore interpret conservative dynamics not as the result of flux balancing, but as the coarse-grained description of an underlying discrete interaction rule in which both conservation and entropy selection are en-coded in the primitive operator. The continuum flux appears only as an emergent representation of this in-teraction, while the primitive dynamics is governed by exact integer transfer.

All simulations use a uniform grid, with identical resolution and CFL number across the compared meth-ods. In the Burgers tests, FQNM and the WENO base-line use the same grid and time step, so differences in shock structure arise from the primitive update mecha-nism.

The Burgers tests use the initial condition u0(x) = sin(2 πx/L ), for which the Hopf–Lax solution is con-structed from the corresponding primitive. The persistence of fine-scale structure through nonlin-ear interaction indicates that shock formation and prop-agation are governed directly by the transfer operator, without requiring a separate Riemann-solver stage. The observed discontinuities are selected and maintained by the monotone integer transfer itself; entropy admissibil-ity is therefore realized at the level of the primitive up-date rather than through reconstructed fluxes. Although the transfer has a first-order upwind form, quantization yields a sharper and more accurately located shock than the unquantized upwind update and the WENO baseline. High-frequency content remains localized without exces-sive numerical diffusion, consistent with the operator-level encoding of both conservation and admissibility. The difference from the sampled Hopf–Lax profile does not indicate a failure of the quantized transfer formula-tion. The Hopf–Lax construction gives the continuum entropy solution, but its direct sampling on a finite grid need not satisfy the intermediate-state consistency condi-tion imposed by the discrete conservative update. Thus the observed discrepancy reflects finite-resolution com-patibility with the discrete update, rather than an error of the method. In this setting, an exact discrete solution is a grid state that satisfies Eq. (3) pointwise at every time step. The intermediate-state condition requires the discrete flux differences to produce a single-valued ad-missible transition across a shock without violating the monotone transfer structure. The Appendix presents a Shu–Osher shock–entropy in-teraction test to illustrate the same operator-level con-struction in a nonlinear system. That example shows that the integer-transfer formulation can sustain coupled nonlinear and high-frequency features in system-level dy-namics, while the present text focuses on the scalar case to expose the core mechanism. 

DISCUSSION 

The present formulation replaces flux-based recon-struction, at the primitive level, with a discrete anti-symmetric transfer operator. Invariance is enforced al-gebraically by the update itself and is therefore indepen-dent of floating-point round-off. Extensions to multidimensional problems and systems follow from the same operator-level construction, al-though they are not developed here in detail. This sug-gests that conservative dynamics may admit a fundamen-tally discrete realization in which flux-based representa-tions are replaced by an exact interaction rule that en-codes both invariance and entropy selection in its primi-tive structure. 

CONCLUSION 

We have introduced an operator-level formulation of conservative dynamics based on exact integer transfer be-tween quantized states. The resulting update conserves exactly in integer arithmetic, avoids round-off drift, and encodes entropy selection through a monotone order-preserving transfer rule. Numerical tests show that this structure preserves high-frequency transport and sharp nonlinear discontinuities, indicating that flux-based con-tinuum descriptions can arise as coarse-grained represen-tations of an exact discrete interaction rule. 

Extension to nonlinear systems 

The integer-transfer formulation introduced in the main text extends directly to nonlinear systems of con-servation laws. In this setting, the state becomes a vector-valued integer field, and the interface transfer is applied componentwise. The antisymmetric structure of the transfer ensures exact conservation for each compo-nent, while intercomponent coupling enters through flux splitting. 4

The Shu–Osher problem combines a strong shock with high-frequency entropy waves and is widely used as a stringent test of numerical dissipation and resolution. Figure 4 shows that the integer-transfer formulation propagates both the shock and the oscillatory structure without significant smoothing at the grid scale. The os-cillatory features remain clearly resolved after interaction with the shock. This behavior indicates that the transfer rule pre-serves fine-scale structure during nonlinear evolution while maintaining exact conservation. This example is not intended as a comprehensive stability study for systems, but rather as representative evidence that the operator-level construction extends naturally beyond the scalar setting.
