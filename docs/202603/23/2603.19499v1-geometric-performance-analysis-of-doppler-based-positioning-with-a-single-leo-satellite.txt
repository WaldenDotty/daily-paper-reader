# Geometric Performance Analysis of Doppler-Based Positioning with a Single LEO Satellite 

Qi Liu, Marc Fernández-Temprado, Antoni Reus-Bergas, Gonzalo Seco-Granados, José A. López-Salcedo 

Abstract—Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites have gained increasing attention as potential signal sources for Positioning, Navigation and Timing (PNT) applications. However, while most existing studies focus on multi-satellite LEO constellations, the fundamental position-ing performance achievable with a single LEO satellite remains less extensively explored. This paper analyzes the geometric characteristics and positioning performance of single-satellite Doppler positioning through a theoret-ical analysis of the Dilution of Precision (DOP) and extensive numerical simulations. The results reveal astrong directional error behavior, with severe error in the cross-track direction but a significantly less error along the satellite track, reflecting an intrinsic geometric limitation of single-satellite LEO positioning. While these features were already identified at the early stages of satellite PNT missions, the present work provides an in-depth analysis and unveils the fundamental limitations and characteristics that could make LEO-based Doppler positioning feasible nowadays, using one single satellite only. In this way, the results of this work not only provide valuable insights into the role of observational geometry in Doppler navigation, but also offer guidance for opti-mizing geometric configurations in future small or single-satellite LEO constellations for strategic applications. Keywords—LEO satellite, positioning, navigation, timing, dilution of precision, single-satellite, Doppler positioning, geometric characteristics.

The advent of the so-called New Space economy has paved the way for the rapid deployment of new LEO constellations, thus prompting researchers to exploit existing LEO signals for PNT applications, or to deploy new dedicated LEO constellations op-timized for PNT [16], [17]. A. Role of Dilution of Precision (DOP) As research on LEO-based PNT continues to expand, it has become clear that beyond measure-ment error mitigation and processing techniques, the geometric configuration of the satellite constellation plays a fundamental role in determining achievable positioning accuracy [24]. This influence is commonly characterized by the Dilution of Precision (DOP), a metric that quantifies how satellite geometry amplifies measurement errors into position domain errors. LEO constellations exhibit rapidly changing spatial geometry and highly dynamic visibility pat-terns, leading to distinct DOP behaviors that vary significantly over time and user location [25], [26]. Understanding these geometric effects is therefore essential for interpreting LEO positioning perfor-mance and for assessing how different constellation designs, orbital parameters, and observation condi-tions shape the overall error characteristics. Tyler et al. [27] conducted a detailed comparison between LEO constellations and GNSS based on aspects such as link budget, number of visible satellites, Geometric Dilution of Precision (GDOP), Horizontal Dilution of Precision (HDOP), Vertical Dilution of Precision (VDOP), and user ranging error. The results show that the LEO constellations have abetter geometry than existing GNSS, while the study did not fully analyze the relationship between DOP and achievable positioning accuracy. Intuitively, DOP reflects how well the satellites are distributed across the sky and is inversely related to the volume of the tetrahedron formed by the user and the visible satellites. In addition, DOP plays a key role in positioning accuracy because the position error is determined by the product of the measurement standard deviation and the DOP. In other words, for the same measurement, a larger DOP will lead to a larger position error, whereas a smaller DOP limits this amplification and yields better positioning accuracy. The conventional DOP formulation is derived from pseudorange-based navigation solutions. In the context of LEO satellite systems, Doppler measure-ments offer a distinct advantage due to their strong sensitivity to the rapid relative motion between the satellites and the user. This makes Doppler-based DOP (DDOP) analysis particularly relevant and of growing interest for assessing the positioning poten-tial of LEO observations. Psiaki [29] and Baron [30] investigated a generalized DDOP analysis suitable for LEO systems and derived an accuracy factor anal-ogous to that used in GNSS, which can be employed to evaluate precision. Ruben et al. [24] provided a comprehensive comparison of code-based DOP and DDOP analysis across existing LEO systems and discussed the optimization steps to follow in building novel LEO-PNT constellations for best positioning performance. Although the analysis addressed sys-tem performance, it did not explicitly evaluate how key geometric variables, including orbital inclination and user location, influence positioning accuracy via DDOP. B. Motivation for Single-Satellite Positioning While large constellations of LEO satellites have received a significant attention in recent years (e.g. Starlink, Amazon LEO, etc.), small constellations are also playing a relevant role in many ongoing experimental and in-orbit demonstrations [31]. Many countries are also considering the deployment of institutional small LEO constellations to support secure communications, which may eventually pro-vide trusted PNT services as well. In parallel, within the private sector, small satellite constellations are increasingly attracting interest for applications such as strategic surveillance, technology demonstration, or as early proof-of-concept systems for future large-scale constellations. Moreover, in real-world operational conditions, users may effectively experience the equivalent of a small LEO constellation due to environmental constraints, such as in indoor environments, urban 3

canyons, or other signal-degraded or denied scenar-ios. In all these situations, positioning based on a very limited number of satellites or, in the extreme case, relying on a single LEO satellite, becomes a relevant capability whose fundamental performance limits are not yet fully understood. These factors prompted our focus on studying the positioning performance of single-satellite LEO cases in this paper, yielding the following contributions. First, we establish a theoretical framework that ana-lytically relates DDOP to single-satellite positioning error, explicitly decoupling the error propagation into along-track and cross-track directions. This model is validated through an analysis of different satellite passes to ensure its reliability under varying geometric conditions. Secondly, based on this vali-dated framework, we conduct a systematic sensitivity analysis to quantify how single-satellite positioning performance is modulated by four critical factors: the observation sampling rate, the start time of the pass, orbital inclination, and the user’s position. Collec-tively, these investigations reveal the error evolution patterns driven by dynamic LEO geometries, serving as a theoretical basis for mitigating geometric singu-larities and finding the best conditions for user’s to implement single-satellite positioning. These findings provide a reference framework for optimizing user-side positioning performance in LEO-PNT scenarios under existing or future satellite constellations. II. Methodology This section introduces the methodology used to assess the performance of single-satellite Doppler-based positioning. First, the signal model for Doppler measurements will be presented, including the single-epoch and the multiple-epoch cases. Then, the geo-metrical problem of solving the user’s position will be formulated by means of Nonlinear Least Squares (NLS) estimation. Finally, the NLS framework will set the grounds for computing the DDOP for the problem at hand. A. The receiver is assumed to be located at [41 .3976 ◦, 2.1497 ◦, 60m] , corresponding to Barcelona, Spain. The simulated dataset is generated at 1 s resolution over 350 s. Satellite trajectories are generated from TLE data of the Orbcomm 108, and only single satellite pass geometry is considered. Unless otherwise stated, all experiments are con-ducted using the simulation setup described above. To more clearly illustrate the relationship between positioning errors and the satellite’s orbital geom-etry, all positioning errors reported are expressed in the Radial-Transverse-Normal (RTN) coordinate frame. The frame is constructed with respect to the satellite that is closest to the user, following the procedure outlined in Fig. 1. In this coordinate frame, the T-axis points along the satellite’s direction of motion (i.e., the along-track direction), while the N-axis corresponds to the cross-track direction. To assess the goodness of the theoretical DDOP obtained in (22)-(25), the values obtained will be compared to the actual positioning errors incurred when solving the user’s position using the iterative WLS framework described in Section II.C. To do so, positioning errors over 1000 Montecarlo simulations 6

will be computed, using two different satellite orbits in order to have some diversity on the impact of the actual satellite pass. Later, average results for all possible passes will be obtained. Doppler measurements are obtained by computing the noise-free Doppler shift based on the satellite and receiver state according to (2), and subsequently corrupting them with zero-mean Gaussian noise to obtain (4). The receiver state estimation is performed using a WLS algorithm, employing all observations within the observation period. To study the accuracy of the theory, the initial position is set to the true value and each trial uses the same satellite geome-try and receiver initial position, while independent realizations of measurement noise. IV. Conclusion In conclusion, we investigated the positioning per-formance of the single-satellite LEO case based on Doppler measurement, with a particular focus on the geometric characteristics and error behavior induced by limited observation geometry. A comprehensive numerical analysis was conducted to examine the consistency between DDOP-based theoretical predic-tions and positioning errors, as well as the influence of key geometric factors, including observation num-bers, sampling times, start time, orbital inclination and user positions. The results demonstrate that single-satellite LEO positioning exhibits pronounced directional charac-teristics in its error distribution. In particular, the cross-track direction is severely weakly constrained, leading to rapid error growth and extreme degrada-tion under certain geometric configurations, with the maximum error reaching 10 8 m. Moreover, the results show that the largest positioning error occurs along the satellite-motion direction, indicating that this behavior reflects an intrinsic geometric limitation. In contrast, the along-track direction is inherently better constrained in single-satellite Doppler posi-tioning, as the Doppler observables provide stronger sensitivity through the projection of the satellite ve-locity onto this direction. As a result, the positioning error is minimized along the satellite trajectory and remains comparatively stable across different ground tracks and orbital configurations. Moreover, based on the numerical results presented in this paper, several practical design guidelines can be concluded for single-satellite LEO-based position-ing: 1) Increasing the sampling interval significantly improves positioning performance up to ap-proximately 60 s, after which the improve-ment becomes limited. Therefore, in practical single-satellite Doppler positioning applications, a sampling interval on the order of tens of seconds (around 60 s) provides a good balance between geometric diversity and measurement availability. 2) Positioning accuracy strongly depends on the temporal placement of the observation window within the satellite pass, the observation interval should therefore be selected to cover the central portion of the satellite pass, with the best perfor-mance obtained when the observation window is centered near the closest satellite–user approach. 3) The satellite orbital geometry also has a sig-nificant impact on positioning performance, the cross-track error becomes largest when the satellite passes directly overhead due to the nearly coplanar observation geometry. Consid-ering both along-track and cross-track perfor-mance, satellite passes with maximum elevation around 60° provide the best overall positioning performance and are therefore preferable for single-satellite Doppler positioning. These findings highlight the fundamental limi-tations and characteristics of single-satellite LEO positioning and provide useful guidance for im-proving observation geometry to achieve enhanced 13 

single-satellite LEO positioning performance. The presented analysis establishes a theoretical and nu-merical foundation for the design and evaluation of more advanced LEO-based PNT architectures, in-cluding multi-satellite configurations and geometry-aware estimation strategies. Future work will extend this framework to real measurement validation.
